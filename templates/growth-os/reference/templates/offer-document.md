# Offer Document -- Output Template

> Signal: `S=(linguistic, spec, commit, markdown, offer-stack)`
>
> The commercial architecture that converts positioning into revenue. This
> document defines exactly what the creator sells, at what price, with what
> guarantee, and why it is economically viable. Every downstream asset --
> VSL, sales page, email sequence, ad -- draws from this document.

## When to Use This Template

Use this template when:
- The `/design-offer` skill is invoked
- Phase 4 of the sequential dependency chain is active
- A validated Positioning Document exists
- The creator needs to design, refine, or restructure their offer

Do NOT use this template when:
- No ICP Document exists or it scores < 80 (redirect to `/build-icp`)
- No Positioning Document exists (redirect to Phase 3 -- positioning must precede offer)
- The request is for offer REPOSITIONING only (existing offer, new angle -- a different workflow)

## Template Structure

### Section 1: Transformation Statement

**Purpose**: The single sentence that captures the complete promise. This is the headline of the offer, the opening of the VSL, the subject line of the launch email. It must be specific, time-bound, and viscerally compelling.

**Contents**:
- Complete the formula:

> **"Go from** [specific current painful state] **to** [specific desired state] **in** [specific timeframe]**."**

- Below the statement, include:
  - **Current State**: Expand on where the audience is now (reference ICP Section 6, top pain)
  - **Desired State**: Expand on where they will be (reference ICP Section 7, dream outcome)
  - **Timeframe**: Realistic and defensible. Must be based on evidence (past client results, industry benchmarks, or logical process timeline).

**Rules**:
- NEVER use vague current/desired states. "Struggling" is vague. "Stuck at $8K/month with no pipeline and doing all the fulfillment yourself" is specific.
- NEVER use unrealistic timeframes to inflate appeal. If results take 90 days, do not say 30.
- ALWAYS ground the timeframe in evidence. State the basis: "Based on [X client results / Y process timeline / Z industry benchmark]."
- The transformation must map directly to the ICP's dream outcome (Section 7) and the positioning's primary benefit.

---

### Section 2: Unique Mechanism

**Purpose**: Carry forward and expand the mechanism from the Positioning Document. In the offer context, the mechanism is the "vehicle" the audience is buying -- the thing that makes the transformation possible.

**Contents**:
- **Name**: The branded mechanism name (must match Positioning Document Section 4)
- **Description**: One paragraph. What it is, how it works, what makes it a system and not just advice.
- **Differentiation**: Why this mechanism is unlike anything else they have tried. Reference ICP Section 8 (What They've Tried & Why It Failed) and connect the mechanism to the failure points of previous solutions.
- **Logic**: The "why it works" argument. A logical chain: "Most people fail because [root cause]. This mechanism works because it [addresses root cause] through [specific approach]." The logic must connect to the Core Belief Statement from the Positioning Document.

**Rules**:
- The mechanism name MUST match the Positioning Document. Inconsistency between documents is a structural failure.
- NEVER describe the mechanism as "comprehensive" or "holistic" -- these are filler words that convey no information. Describe what it specifically does.
- Differentiation must reference specific failed alternatives from the ICP, not generic "other approaches"
- The logic chain must be internally consistent and traceable from root cause to mechanism to outcome

---

### Section 3: Value Equation Score

**Purpose**: Apply the Value Equation to quantify the offer's perceived value. This framework ensures the offer is architected to maximize the buyer's perception of value -- not just the creator's.

**Contents**:
- **Dream Outcome**: What specific, measurable result does the buyer get? (e.g., "Go from $8K to $25K/month in revenue" or "Build a 90-day content system that runs without you"). Must be tangible and verifiable.
- **Perceived Likelihood of Achievement**: What proof exists that buyers will actually achieve this? Rate evidence strength:
  - Strong: Multiple case studies with similar audience, verified results
  - Moderate: Creator's own results, partial case studies, logical argument
  - Weak: Theoretical framework only, no client results yet
  - Include what proof assets exist or need to be created
- **Time Delay**: How long until results? Shorter is more valuable. Express in specific units (days, weeks, months). Include both "first win" timeline (when they see initial progress) and "full result" timeline.
- **Effort Required**: What does the buyer have to do? Lower effort = higher value. Be honest. Include time commitment per week and any prerequisites.
- **Score**: Express the equation:

> **Value = (Dream Outcome x Perceived Likelihood) / (Time Delay x Effort Required)**

Assign each dimension a 1-10 rating with justification. Calculate and display the composite score. A score above 5.0 indicates strong perceived value. Below 3.0 requires offer redesign.

**Rules**:
- NEVER inflate the score. Every dimension rating must include its justification.
- Perceived Likelihood must honestly assess what proof exists TODAY, not what could exist in the future
- Time Delay and Effort must be stated from the BUYER's perspective, not the creator's
- ALWAYS include both the "first win" and "full result" timelines -- early wins reduce perceived risk

---

### Section 4: Offer Tiers

**Purpose**: Define 1-3 pricing tiers that create a value ladder. Each tier serves a different segment of the ICP based on budget, commitment level, and desired speed of results.

**Contents**:
- For each tier (1-3), document:
  - **Tier Name**: A branded name that communicates value, not just a level (e.g., "Foundation Builder" not "Basic," "Growth Accelerator" not "Pro")
  - **Delivery Model**: DIY (self-paced, do it yourself) / DWY (done with you, coaching + implementation support) / DFY (done for you, full service). Be explicit about what the creator delivers vs. what the buyer does.
  - **Duration**: How long does this tier last? Access period, coaching period, and any ongoing support window.
  - **Core Deliverables**: List 5-8 deliverables per tier. EVERY deliverable must be framed as an OUTCOME, not a feature.
    - WRONG: "8 video modules"
    - RIGHT: "The complete [Mechanism Name] implementation -- 8 guided sessions that take you from [start] to [end]"
    - WRONG: "Weekly group calls"
    - RIGHT: "Weekly hot-seat coaching where you get your specific [problem] diagnosed and solved in real time"
  - **Price + Price Anchor**: The actual price AND the anchored value. (e.g., "Investment: $2,997 — Value of components purchased separately: $12,400"). The anchor must be defensible -- each component's individual value must be justifiable.
  - **Timeline to Results**: When can someone in this tier expect their first measurable result? Be specific.

**Rules**:
- NEVER frame deliverables as features. Every deliverable must answer "so that [buyer outcome]"
- NEVER price without anchoring. The anchor provides context that makes the price feel rational.
- Price anchors must be DEFENSIBLE -- each component's individual value must be traceable to market rates or comparable products
- ALWAYS differentiate tiers on VALUE DELIVERED, not just "more stuff." Higher tiers should deliver faster results, more personalization, or higher-touch implementation -- not just additional modules.
- If using multiple tiers, the middle tier should be the target. Price the lowest tier to attract, the middle to convert, and the highest to anchor.

---

### Section 5: Bonus Stack

**Purpose**: Bonuses increase perceived value without increasing the core commitment. Each bonus should solve a DIFFERENT problem than the core offer -- complementary, not redundant.

**Contents**:
- 3-5 bonuses, each with:
  - **Name**: Branded, specific, outcome-oriented. (e.g., "The Client Attraction Script Pack" not "Bonus Templates")
  - **Problem It Solves**: A specific problem ADJACENT to the core offer. (e.g., if the core offer teaches offer design, a bonus might solve "but how do I describe this offer in a sales conversation?")
  - **Perceived Value**: A dollar amount with defensible justification. (e.g., "$497 value — based on comparable standalone products: [Product X] sells their script pack for $397, [Product Y] for $597")
  - **Why It's Included**: The strategic reason. (e.g., "We include this because the #2 reason clients stall after building their offer is they don't know how to present it. This eliminates that bottleneck.")

**Rules**:
- EVERY bonus must solve a DIFFERENT problem than the core offer. Bonuses that duplicate core deliverables dilute value rather than adding it.
- Perceived value must be defensible with market comparisons. "$10,000 value" for a PDF checklist is noise.
- NEVER include more than 5 bonuses. Beyond 5, the stack creates cognitive overload and signals desperation.
- ALWAYS explain why each bonus is included. "Because we want to over-deliver" is not a reason. "Because 63% of our clients hit [specific bottleneck] and this eliminates it" is a reason.

---

### Section 6: Guarantee Design

**Purpose**: Remove the buyer's risk. The guarantee is not a marketing gimmick -- it is a structural element that transfers risk from buyer to seller, making the purchase decision easier.

**Contents**:
- **Type**: Classify the guarantee:
  - **Performance guarantee**: "If you don't achieve [specific result]..."
  - **ROI guarantee**: "If you don't make back your investment within [timeframe]..."
  - **Completion guarantee**: "If you complete all [steps/modules] and don't see [result]..."
  - **Reversal guarantee**: "Full refund within [timeframe], no questions asked"
- **Statement**: The exact guarantee language. Must be a complete, unambiguous sentence. (e.g., "If you implement the full [Mechanism Name] and don't add at least $5,000/month in new revenue within 90 days, we'll refund 100% of your investment AND give you an additional [bonus] for wasting your time.")
- **Conditions** (if any): What must the buyer do to qualify for the guarantee? (e.g., "Complete all 8 modules, attend 6 of 8 coaching calls, submit implementation homework"). Conditions must be reasonable and trackable.
- **Confidence Reasoning**: Why the creator can offer this guarantee. What about their mechanism, track record, or process makes this a safe bet? (e.g., "We can offer this because our completion rate is 87% and of those who complete, 94% hit the revenue target within 90 days.")

**Rules**:
- ALWAYS make the guarantee SPECIFIC. "Satisfaction guaranteed" is meaningless. Name the result, the timeframe, and the remedy.
- ALWAYS make the guarantee TIME-BOUND. Open-ended guarantees are operationally dangerous and signal low confidence.
- NEVER design a guarantee the creator cannot operationally honor. If they cannot track completion or verify results, the guarantee type must change.
- Conditions must be REASONABLE. If the conditions are so onerous that no one could qualify, the guarantee is performative, not real.
- The confidence reasoning must cite evidence (completion rates, success rates, past guarantee claims) or state "pre-launch estimate" if no historical data exists.

### Section 7: Pricing Rationale

**Purpose**: Frame the price in three ways that make it feel rational and inevitable. Price is always relative -- the job is to provide the right reference points.

**Contents**:
- **Cost of Inaction**: What does it cost the buyer to NOT solve this problem? Calculate the monthly and annual cost of staying where they are. (e.g., "Every month without a systemized offer costs you $5K-$15K in revenue you should be capturing. Over 12 months, that's $60K-$180K left on the table. This program costs less than ONE month of inaction.")
- **Comparison Stack**: What would it cost to assemble the same outcome from separate purchases? List 4-7 components with individual market prices. Total must be >= 3x the actual offer price. (e.g., "Private consulting: $5K. Course on [topic]: $997. Templates: $497. Community access: $1,200/year. Coaching calls: $500/session x 8 = $4,000. Total: $11,694. Your investment: $2,997.")
- **ROI Timeline**: When does the investment pay for itself? Calculate the break-even scenario. (e.g., "At $2,997 investment, you need ONE additional client at $3,000+ to break even. Most clients land their first new client within 30 days of implementation. Break-even: Month 1.")

**Rules**:
- Cost of inaction must be CALCULATED, not asserted. Show the math.
- Comparison stack items must be real, verifiable products or services at real market prices. Do not inflate.
- ROI timeline must be based on a realistic scenario, not the best-case outlier
- NEVER make the pricing rationale feel manipulative. The goal is to provide genuine context for the investment, not to pressure.
- All three frames (inaction, comparison, ROI) are required. Missing any one leaves a gap in the buyer's reasoning.

---

### Section 8: Urgency Element

**Purpose**: Give the buyer a legitimate reason to act now rather than later. Real urgency accelerates decisions. Manufactured urgency destroys trust.

**Contents**:
- **Mechanism**: What creates the urgency?
  - **Capacity**: Limited spots due to delivery model constraints (coaching bandwidth, cohort size)
  - **Seasonal/Timing**: Market window, industry cycle, calendar-driven relevance
  - **Price**: Price increasing on a specific date with a stated reason
  - **Bonus Expiration**: Time-limited bonuses that genuinely will not be available later
  - **Market Timing**: External market conditions that make now the optimal window
- **Why It's Real**: The honest reason this urgency exists. (e.g., "We cap each cohort at 20 because the coaching model requires live hot seats, and beyond 20, quality degrades." NOT "Only 20 spots because we said so.")
- **Exact Language**: The copy-ready urgency statement for use in sales assets. (e.g., "This cohort opens [date] with 20 spots. We've filled the last three cohorts in under 72 hours. If you're reading this and spots are still available, the link below works. If not, join the waitlist for the next cohort.")

**Rules**:
- NEVER manufacture urgency that is not real. Fake countdown timers, fake scarcity, and fake price increases destroy trust and violate the audience's intelligence.
- ALWAYS explain WHY the urgency exists. Legitimate scarcity has a logical reason.
- The urgency mechanism must be OPERATIONALLY REAL -- the creator must actually enforce it
- If no natural urgency exists, it is acceptable to state "No artificial urgency. The offer is available on an ongoing basis. Urgency comes from the cost of inaction (Section 7)."

### Section 9: Belief Stack

**Purpose**: Identify the 3-5 beliefs the prospect must hold in order to buy. If any belief is missing, the sale stalls. This stack drives the pre-sale content strategy and the sales page architecture.

**Contents**:
- 3-5 beliefs, each with:
  - **The Belief**: One clear sentence. (e.g., "My offer -- not my traffic -- is what's holding me back.")
  - **Current State**: Do they already believe this? (Already installed / Partially installed / Not yet installed). Reference ICP awareness level and limiting belief diagnosis.
  - **Installation Method**: What content, proof, or story installs this belief? Be specific. (e.g., "Case study email showing client who 3x'd revenue without changing traffic. Supporting data from research brief showing conversion rates vs. traffic correlation.")
  - **Depth Layer**: Which layer of belief does this operate at?
    - **Surface**: Intellectual agreement ("that makes sense")
    - **Behavior**: Changes what they do ("I should try this")
    - **Identity**: Changes who they see themselves as ("I'm someone who invests in systems")
    - **Destiny**: Changes their sense of what's possible ("This could actually work for me")

**Rules**:
- NEVER list more than 5 beliefs. If you need more, the offer is trying to do too much.
- ALWAYS assess the current state honestly. Marking everything as "not yet installed" when the audience is Solution Aware is inaccurate.
- Installation methods must be ACTIONABLE -- not "create content" but "write a case study email about [specific client result] that demonstrates [specific belief]"
- Belief #1 should always be the Core Belief from the Positioning Document. The remaining beliefs support it.
- Deeper belief layers (Identity, Destiny) require more proof and more time to install than surface beliefs. Acknowledge this in the installation method.

### Section 10: Objection Handling

**Purpose**: Pre-build the reframes for the top 4 objections this audience will raise. These become sales page sections, email sequences, FAQ answers, and sales call responses.

**Contents**:
- Top 4 objections (pull directly from ICP Section 9), each with:
  - **Objection**: The verbatim objection in the buyer's language
  - **What They Really Mean**: The real concern beneath the surface objection (from ICP Section 9, Real Objection layer)
  - **Reframe**: The strategic response that addresses the real concern. 2-4 sentences. Must include at least one proof point (case study, data, guarantee reference, testimonial).

**Suggested objection categories** (at minimum, cover these four):
1. **Price objection**: "It's too expensive" / "I can't afford it"
2. **Fit objection**: "Will this work for my specific situation?"
3. **Timing objection**: "I need to think about it" / "Not the right time"
4. **Trust objection**: "I've been burned before" / "How do I know this is different?"

**Rules**:
- EVERY reframe must include at least one concrete proof point. Generic reassurance ("trust us") is not a reframe.
- NEVER dismiss the objection. Acknowledge it first, then reframe. The structure is: Acknowledge the concern → Reframe the underlying issue → Provide proof → Connect to the offer element that resolves it.
- Reframes must reference specific offer elements (guarantee, mechanism, case study, bonus) -- not general arguments
- Pull objections directly from the ICP document. Do not invent new ones.

---

### Section 11: Economics Validation

**Purpose**: Verify that the offer is economically viable as a business, not just attractive as a pitch. An offer that sounds great but loses money is worse than no offer.

**Contents**:
- **Target LTV (Lifetime Value)**: The total revenue expected from one customer over their lifetime. Include calculation methodology. (e.g., "Core program: $2,997 + average upsell rate of 30% to next tier at $7,500 = weighted LTV of $5,247")
- **Target CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost)**: The expected cost to acquire one customer. Include channel assumptions. (e.g., "Paid: $150 CPA on Meta at 2% conversion on $2,997 offer = $300 CAC. Organic: estimated $50 CAC blended.")
- **LTV:CAC Ratio**: Must be >= 3:1 for the offer to be viable. Calculate and display. If below 3:1, flag the specific lever that needs adjustment (raise price, reduce CAC, increase upsell rate, reduce churn).
- **Break-Even Point**: How many customers at what price to cover fixed costs? Express in units and time. (e.g., "At $2,997 per client and $3,000/month in fixed costs, break-even is 1 client per month.")
- **Margin Analysis**: What is the gross margin per client after delivery costs? Include: price - delivery cost = margin. (e.g., "Revenue: $2,997. Delivery cost (platform, coaching time, support): ~$600. Gross margin: $2,397 (80%).")

**Rules**:
- LTV:CAC MUST be >= 3:1. If it is not, the offer document status remains `draft` and the quality gate fails. Include specific recommendations for achieving the ratio.
- NEVER present economics without showing the calculation methodology. Numbers without math are assertions, not validation.
- ALWAYS include delivery cost in the margin analysis. Revenue without cost analysis is vanity math.
- If the creator has no historical data (pre-launch), state all assumptions explicitly and mark the economics as "projected, requires validation"
- Break-even must be expressed in a specific, actionable unit (X clients in Y months)

### Section 12: Offer One-Pager

**Purpose**: A compressed summary of the entire offer for downstream agent consumption. This is stored in `company.yaml` and `reference/offers.md` and loaded by every agent that needs to reference the offer (VSL agent, email agent, ad agent, funnel agent).

**Contents**:
- **Who it's for**: 1 sentence. (Pull from ICP summary)
- **What they get**: 1 sentence. (The core transformation + mechanism)
- **The mechanism**: 1 sentence. (The named mechanism and its key differentiator)
- **The price**: Range if multiple tiers. (e.g., "$997 - $4,997 depending on tier")
- **The guarantee**: 1 sentence. (The guarantee statement from Section 6)
- **The CTA**: 1 sentence. (What action to take: book a call, join waitlist, enroll now)

**Rules**:
- NEVER exceed 200 words total for the one-pager
- EVERY sentence must be independently meaningful -- no sentence should require another sentence for context
- The one-pager must be SELF-CONTAINED. An agent loading only this section should have enough context to write a basic ad, email, or social post about the offer.
- Language must match the audience's vocabulary (from ICP Section 12), not internal marketing jargon

## Shannon Constraints

| Constraint | Limit |
|-----------|-------|
| Total document length | 3,000 - 6,000 words |
| Transformation Statement (Section 1) | 1 sentence formula + max 150 words supporting |
| Offer One-Pager (Section 12) | Maximum 200 words |
| Deliverables per tier | 5-8, each outcome-framed |
| Bonuses | 3-5, each solving a different adjacent problem |
| Beliefs | 3-5, each with 4 fields |
| Objection reframes | Top 4, each with proof point |
| Individual section | No section exceeds 600 words |

The offer document must be dense enough that a downstream agent can build a complete sales page, VSL script, or email sequence from it alone -- but not so long that loading it consumes excessive context. Precision over volume.

## Quality Gate Criteria

This offer document PASSES the quality gate when:

1. **Value stack perceived value >= 10x price**: Total value of core deliverables + bonuses (at defensible market rates) is at least 10x the asking price
2. **LTV:CAC >= 3:1**: Economics validation shows a viable business, not just an attractive offer
3. **Guarantee is specific and time-bound**: Names the result, the timeframe, and the remedy
4. **Every deliverable is framed as an outcome**: No feature-only descriptions. "8 modules" fails. "A complete [system] that takes you from [A] to [B]" passes.
5. **Mechanism is named and differentiated**: Matches the Positioning Document. Has a branded name. Is specifically different from named competitors.
6. **Transformation statement is specific**: Names the current state, desired state, and timeframe with evidence for all three
7. **All 12 sections are present** and substantively completed
8. **Pricing rationale includes all three frames**: Cost of inaction, comparison stack, ROI timeline
9. **One-pager is <= 200 words** and self-contained
10. **Belief stack connects to Positioning Document's core belief**

This offer document FAILS the quality gate when:

1. LTV:CAC ratio is below 3:1 (document remains in draft status until economics are fixed)
2. Value stack perceived value is less than 10x the price
3. Guarantee is vague ("satisfaction guaranteed") or has no timeframe
4. Deliverables are listed as features without outcome framing
5. Mechanism is unnamed or uses the same description as a competitor's approach
6. Economics section presents numbers without calculation methodology
7. Bonuses duplicate core deliverables instead of solving adjacent problems
8. Urgency is manufactured rather than operationally real
9. Objection reframes lack specific proof points
10. The offer document could apply to a different creator's business with minimal changes (specificity failure)
