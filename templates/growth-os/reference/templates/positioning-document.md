# Positioning Document -- Output Template

> Signal: `S=(linguistic, spec, commit, markdown, positioning-document)`
>
> The strategic bridge between knowing WHO you serve (ICP) and designing
> WHAT you sell (Offer). Positioning defines the mental real estate you
> occupy in the audience's mind. Get it wrong and the best offer in the
> world sells to no one.

## When to Use This Template

Use this template when:
- Phase 3 of the sequential dependency chain is active
- A validated ICP Document exists (completeness score >= 80)
- The creator needs to define or refine how they are positioned against competitors
- The offer architecture agent requires a positioning foundation before designing the offer

Do NOT use this template when:
- No ICP Document exists or it scores < 80 (redirect to `/build-icp` first -- hard dependency)
- The request is for competitor-specific analysis only (use `competitor-battlecard.md`)
- The creator has a validated positioning document and has not changed their ICP, niche, or competitive landscape

## Template Structure

### Section 1: Positioning Statement

**Purpose**: One structured sentence that captures who you serve, what you do, and why you win. This is the strategic anchor for all messaging.

**Contents**:
- Complete the positioning formula:

> **For** [target audience] **who** [primary pain / situation],
> [product/brand name] **is the** [category]
> **that** [primary benefit / outcome]
> **because** [unique reason to believe / mechanism].

- Below the statement, include:
  - **Audience**: Reference the ICP document summary (link, not repeat)
  - **Pain**: The single most acute pain from the ICP (Section 6, top-ranked)
  - **Category**: What shelf this sits on in the buyer's mind (see Section 2)
  - **Benefit**: The primary outcome, stated as a result, not a feature
  - **Reason to Believe**: The unique mechanism or proof that makes this credible

**Rules**:
- The positioning statement must be a SINGLE sentence following the formula. No paragraphs.
- ALWAYS use the audience's language for the pain component (pull from ICP Section 12)
- NEVER use superlatives without evidence ("the best," "the most comprehensive," "the only" -- unless verifiably true)
- The statement must be TESTABLE: show it to 10 prospects and they should immediately understand what this is, who it is for, and why it is different. If any of those three are unclear, rewrite.

---

### Section 2: Category Design

**Purpose**: Define the competitive frame. Are you entering an existing category, or creating a new one? This decision shapes everything from pricing to content strategy.

**Contents**:
- **Category Choice**: One of three strategic moves:
  - **Enter an existing category**: Compete head-to-head (e.g., "online course for Facebook ads"). Requires clear differentiation on a dimension the market cares about.
  - **Subcategory creation**: Narrow an existing category to own a niche (e.g., "Facebook ads specifically for local service businesses"). Requires a defensible reason the subcategory deserves its own label.
  - **Category creation**: Invent a new category (e.g., "Revenue Architecture" instead of "sales training"). Requires educating the market on why this category exists. Highest upside, highest effort.
- **Category Label**: What do you call this thing? The name must be instantly parsed by the target audience. If they have to ask "what is that?", the label needs work.
- **Old Vehicles Being Replaced**: What existing solutions does this category supersede? Name them explicitly. (e.g., "This replaces: generic online courses, one-on-one coaching that doesn't scale, DIY from YouTube")
- **Why the Old Vehicles Fail**: Connect to ICP Section 8 (What They've Tried & Why It Failed). The category story is: "The old way doesn't work because X. This new category exists because Y."

**Rules**:
- ALWAYS name the old vehicles being replaced -- positioning is relative, not absolute
- NEVER create a new category without a clear explanation of why existing categories are insufficient. Category creation for novelty's sake is noise.
- The category label must pass the "explain it to a stranger in 5 seconds" test
- Connect old vehicle failures to evidence from the ICP and Market Research Brief

---

### Section 3: Core Belief Statement

**Purpose**: Identify the single belief the market must hold for your offer to make sense. If they believe this, your offer is the logical conclusion. If they don't, no amount of marketing works.

**Contents**:
- **The Core Belief**: One sentence. (e.g., "The reason most agency owners plateau at $20K/month is not a lead gen problem -- it's an offer architecture problem." or "You don't need more traffic. You need a system that converts the traffic you already have.")
- **Why It Must Be True**: The logical argument supporting this belief. 2-3 sentences.
- **Evidence That Supports It**: Data points, case studies, market observations from the Research Brief that validate this belief.
- **What Happens If They Don't Believe It**: What competing belief keeps them stuck? What do they currently believe instead? (e.g., "They currently believe 'I just need more leads' -- and as long as they believe that, they'll keep buying lead gen tools instead of fixing their offer.")
- **How to Install This Belief**: The content and proof strategy that shifts them from their current belief to your core belief. (e.g., "Content sequence: 1) Show data on conversion rates vs. traffic volume. 2) Case study of client who 3x'd revenue without adding traffic. 3) Framework that names the real problem.")

**Rules**:
- ALWAYS state the competing belief (what they believe now). Without it, you don't know what you're replacing.
- The core belief must be SPECIFIC to this niche. "Working smarter is better than working harder" is generic. It fails.
- NEVER require more than one core belief. If you need multiple beliefs, the positioning is unfocused. Pick the one that, once installed, makes all others easier to install.
- The belief installation strategy must include at least one piece of concrete evidence (case study, data point, or demonstrable proof)

---

### Section 4: Unique Mechanism

**Purpose**: Name and define the proprietary system, process, or approach that delivers the promised transformation. The mechanism is what makes the audience believe "this one is different."

**Contents**:
- **Name**: A branded name for the mechanism. (e.g., "The Revenue Architecture Method," "The 3-Layer Funnel System," "The Client Clarity Framework"). Must be memorable, specific, and not already taken.
- **What It Is**: One paragraph describing the mechanism in plain language. What does it do? How does it work at a high level?
- **Why It's Different**: How does this mechanism differ from the approaches competitors use? Reference specific competitor approaches from the Market Research Brief or Competitor Battlecards.
- **Why It Works**: The logical or evidence-based argument for why this mechanism produces results. Connect to the core belief statement -- the mechanism is the "how" that makes the belief actionable.

**Rules**:
- ALWAYS give the mechanism a proper name. Unnamed mechanisms are unmemorable and indefensible.
- NEVER describe the mechanism in generic terms. "A proven system" is not a mechanism. "A 4-phase diagnostic that identifies your offer's conversion bottleneck in 90 minutes" is a mechanism.
- The mechanism must be DIFFERENTIATED -- if a competitor could describe their approach with identical words, the mechanism is not unique enough
- Why It Works must connect logically to the core belief. If the core belief is "it's an offer problem, not a traffic problem," the mechanism must address offer architecture, not traffic generation.

---

### Section 5: Competitive Differentiation

**Purpose**: Make the differences between you and competitors explicit, specific, and evidence-based. This is not about being "better" -- it is about being DIFFERENT on dimensions the market cares about.

**Contents**:
- 3-5 specific points of differentiation, each with:
  - **Dimension**: What aspect of the offer/delivery/experience is different (e.g., delivery model, specificity of niche, speed to results, level of access, pricing structure)
  - **Their Approach**: How competitors handle this dimension (reference specific competitors by name)
  - **Your Approach**: How you handle it differently
  - **Why It Matters**: Why the TARGET AUDIENCE (not you) cares about this difference. Connect to a pain point or desire from the ICP.
  - **Evidence**: Proof that this difference is real and valued (testimonials, data, competitor reviews complaining about the old way)

**Rules**:
- EVERY differentiator must reference a specific competitor or competitor behavior -- not a generic "others do X"
- NEVER claim differentiation on a dimension the market does not care about. Being different is not inherently valuable -- being different on something that MATTERS is valuable.
- ALWAYS connect each differentiator back to a specific ICP pain point or desire
- Minimum 3 differentiators. If you cannot identify 3, the positioning needs fundamental rework.
- NEVER use "better" as a differentiator. Better is subjective and undefendable. Different is specific and demonstrable.

---

### Section 6: Brand Narrative

**Purpose**: The origin story that connects the creator's journey to the audience's struggle. This is not a biography -- it is a strategic narrative that builds credibility, empathy, and trust.

**Contents**:
- **The Struggle**: When did the creator face the same (or analogous) problem their audience faces? What was at stake?
- **The Discovery**: What did they discover, build, or realize that changed their trajectory? This should naturally introduce the mechanism.
- **The Mission**: Why are they now helping others with this? What drives them beyond money?
- **The Connection Point**: The specific moment or detail that makes the audience think "this person understands me." Must be grounded in shared experience, not performative empathy.

**Rules**:
- NEVER fabricate or exaggerate the narrative. The story must be true and verifiable.
- ALWAYS include a specific struggle that mirrors the audience's current situation -- this is the empathy bridge
- The discovery should naturally lead to the mechanism (Section 4) -- the narrative and the mechanism must be coherent
- NEVER make the story purely about the creator. The audience is the hero. The creator is the guide who has walked the path before them.
- Keep it concise. This is not an autobiography -- it is a strategic narrative. 200-400 words maximum.

---

### Section 7: Messaging Hierarchy

**Purpose**: Define the priority order of messages from most important to supporting. Every piece of content, ad, email, and sales page draws from this hierarchy.

**Contents**:
- **Primary Message**: The single most important thing to communicate. If the audience hears nothing else, they hear this. One sentence. (e.g., "You don't need more leads -- you need an offer people can't refuse.")
- **Supporting Messages** (3-5): Messages that reinforce, expand, or prove the primary message. Each with:
  - The message (one sentence)
  - What it supports (which aspect of the primary message it reinforces)
  - Where it deploys best (e.g., "headline for cold traffic," "email sequence middle," "sales page objection handling")
- **Proof Points** (3-5): Specific evidence that makes the messages credible. Each with:
  - The proof (specific result, data point, testimonial, case study)
  - Which message it supports
  - Format recommendation (e.g., "screenshot testimonial," "before/after metrics," "video case study")

**Rules**:
- ALWAYS have exactly ONE primary message. If you cannot choose, the positioning is unfocused.
- Supporting messages must SUPPORT the primary, not introduce new themes
- Every proof point must be SPECIFIC. "Great results" is not proof. "$47K in 60 days from implementing the mechanism" is proof.
- NEVER create messaging that contradicts the core belief statement (Section 3). All messages must be philosophically coherent.
- Proof points must be real or clearly marked as "needed" if they do not yet exist

---

### Section 8: Anti-Positioning

**Purpose**: Define what you are NOT. Exclusion is as powerful as inclusion in positioning. Saying who you don't serve and what you refuse to do sharpens the signal for those you DO serve.

**Contents**:
- **What You Are NOT**: 3-5 explicit negations. (e.g., "This is NOT another generic marketing course," "We are NOT a done-for-you agency," "This is NOT for people looking for get-rich-quick shortcuts")
- **Who You Don't Serve**: Specific audience segments you explicitly exclude. (e.g., "Not for beginners who haven't validated a business idea yet," "Not for people unwilling to implement," "Not for businesses below $5K/month revenue")
- **What You Refuse to Do**: Principled positions that differentiate you. (e.g., "We don't teach tactics that expire in 6 months," "We don't do cookie-cutter templates," "We don't promise overnight results")
- **Why the Exclusions Matter**: How each exclusion strengthens the offer for those who DO qualify. (e.g., "By excluding beginners, we can skip 101 material and go deep on advanced strategy -- which is what our ICP actually wants")

**Rules**:
- EVERY exclusion must make the included audience feel MORE confident, not less. Anti-positioning attracts by repelling.
- NEVER be vague in exclusions. "Not for everyone" is meaningless. "Not for agency owners billing less than $10K/month" is specific and useful.
- Anti-positioning must be GENUINE, not performative. If you would actually take someone's money despite what you claim to refuse, it is not real anti-positioning.
- ALWAYS explain why each exclusion benefits the ideal customer

## Shannon Constraints

| Constraint | Limit |
|-----------|-------|
| Total document length | 1,500 - 3,000 words |
| Positioning Statement (Section 1) | One sentence (formula), plus supporting fields totaling max 150 words |
| Core Belief Statement (Section 3) | Max 300 words |
| Brand Narrative (Section 6) | 200-400 words |
| Messaging Hierarchy | Primary message: 1 sentence. Supporting: max 5. Proof: max 5. |
| Competitive Differentiation | 3-5 differentiators, each max 100 words |
| Anti-Positioning | 3-5 exclusions per subsection, each 1-2 sentences |

Positioning documents are compression instruments. Every word must earn its place. If a section can be said in fewer words without losing meaning, compress it. The audience for this document is the offer architecture agent and the creator -- both need precision, not volume.

## Quality Gate Criteria

This positioning document PASSES the quality gate when:

1. **Positioning statement follows the exact formula** and is a single testable sentence
2. **Mechanism is named** with a branded, specific, differentiated name
3. **Core belief is singular** -- one belief, not a list of beliefs
4. **Competitive differentiation references specific competitors by name** (minimum 3 differentiators)
5. **Every differentiator connects to a specific ICP pain or desire**
6. **Messaging hierarchy has exactly one primary message** with supporting messages that reinforce (not diverge from) it
7. **Anti-positioning includes specific exclusions** that strengthen the offer for the included audience
8. **Brand narrative is grounded in true, verifiable experience**
9. **All sections reference ICP and Market Research data** -- no assertions without upstream evidence

This positioning document FAILS the quality gate when:

1. Positioning statement uses superlatives without evidence ("the best," "the only")
2. Mechanism is unnamed or described in generic terms ("a proven system")
3. Core belief could apply to any niche ("working smarter not harder")
4. Competitive differentiation says "better" without specifying "different on what dimension"
5. Messaging hierarchy has multiple competing primary messages
6. Anti-positioning is vague ("not for everyone")
7. Brand narrative is fabricated or not connected to the audience's struggle
8. The document could be used for a different business with minimal changes (specificity failure)
9. No reference to ICP data or Market Research findings anywhere in the document
