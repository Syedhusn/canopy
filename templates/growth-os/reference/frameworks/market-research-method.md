# Market Research Method

**What it is:** The methodology for finding truth in markets -- how to gather evidence, what sources to trust, how to cross-validate findings, and how to separate signal from noise.
**When to apply:** When building a Market Research Brief. When validating assumptions in an ICP Document. When a creator claims to "know their audience" but has no evidence. When entering a new market or launching a new offer.

---

## The Evidence Hierarchy

Not all data is equal. What people DO outweighs what people SAY. Rank evidence by reliability:

| Rank | Evidence Type | Reliability | Why |
|------|-------------|-------------|-----|
| 1 | **Paid** (what they bought) | Highest | Money is the ultimate truth signal. Past purchases reveal real priorities. |
| 2 | **Spent** (where they invest time) | Very high | Time allocation reveals actual values, not stated values. |
| 3 | **Said** (testimonials, reviews, complaints) | High | Unprompted language is gold. But people perform for audiences. |
| 4 | **Searched** (search queries, questions asked) | Medium-high | Shows intent but not commitment. Still useful for language mining. |
| 5 | **Engaged** (likes, comments, shares) | Medium | Shows interest but not purchase intent. Engagement is not revenue. |
| 6 | **Surveyed** (direct questions, polls) | Lowest | People lie in surveys. They give aspirational answers, not truthful ones. Useful only when triangulated with higher-tier evidence. |

## 12 Data Sources, Prioritized

### Tier 1: Transactional Evidence (what they actually pay for)

1. **Competitor sales pages and pricing.** What is being sold, at what price, with what positioning? The market votes with wallets -- active offers reveal demand.
2. **Refund patterns and complaint threads.** Where offers fail reveals what the market actually needs versus what it is being sold.
3. **Affiliate/JV landscape.** What products are being promoted, at what commission rates? High commissions signal high margins, which signal strong demand.

### Tier 2: Behavioral Evidence (what they do without prompting)

4. **Forum and community threads.** Reddit, Facebook groups, Discord servers, niche forums. Unprompted questions and complaints are the richest source of voice-of-customer language.
5. **Search volume and question data.** Google Trends, AnswerThePublic, Quora, YouTube search suggestions. Shows what people actively seek answers to.
6. **Content engagement patterns.** Which topics in the niche get disproportionate engagement? Which get ignored despite seeming important?

### Tier 3: Stated Evidence (what they claim)

7. **Reviews on competitor products.** Amazon, course platforms, coaching testimonials. 1-star and 5-star reviews reveal the most -- what delights and what enrages.
8. **Social media commentary.** Comments on competitor content, influencer posts, niche hashtags. Language patterns here feed directly into VOC sections.
9. **Interview and podcast transcripts.** When target audience members speak at length, they reveal belief structures and emotional drivers that short-form content hides.

### Tier 4: Contextual Evidence (market-level signals)

10. **Industry reports and trend data.** Market size, growth rates, technological shifts. Provides the macro context for market state assessment.
11. **Regulatory and cultural shifts.** New laws, social movements, technological disruptions that create or destroy demand.
12. **Creator/influencer landscape mapping.** Who is entering and leaving the space? New entrants signal opportunity. Exits signal contraction.

## The Cross-Validation Rule

No finding is valid from a single source. **Three or more independent sources must confirm a finding before it becomes actionable intelligence.** A pattern that appears in competitor reviews, community threads, AND search data is a real signal. A pattern that appears only in a survey is suspect.

## Signal vs. Noise Filters

| Source Type | Signal | Noise |
|-------------|--------|-------|
| Forums/communities | Repeated questions, emotional language, specific frustrations | One-off complaints, trolling, off-topic rants |
| Reviews | Patterns across multiple reviews, specific outcome language | Individual outliers, obviously fake reviews |
| Search data | Sustained search volume, rising trends | Seasonal spikes, news-driven anomalies |
| Social engagement | Comments asking "how?", saves/shares, DM requests | Likes without depth, bot engagement |
| Surveys | Consistent patterns across demographics | Aspirational answers, social desirability bias |
| Competitor behavior | What they keep doing (validated through revenue) | What they try once and drop (failed experiments) |

## Two-Phase Approach

### Phase 1: Broad Scan (Market Research Brief)

Cast a wide net. The goal is market-level understanding, not individual psychology.

- Assess market state (expanding/contracting/standing still)
- Diagnose maturity stage (naive/aware/skeptical/exhausted)
- Map competitive landscape (who, what, at what price, what gaps)
- Validate economics (path to 3:1 LTV:CAC)
- Produce Go/No-Go recommendation

Time investment: 2-4 hours of focused research. Output: 800-1200 words.

### Phase 2: Deep Dive (ICP Document)

Narrow focus. The goal is individual-level psychological profiling.

- Demographic and situational specificity
- Psychological architecture (limiting beliefs, awareness, identity)
- Four-layer pain excavation (perceived, real, private, secret fears)
- Capability and resource audit with numeric ratings
- Buying intelligence with actual VOC phrases
- Strategic implications for offer architecture

Time investment: 4-8 hours of focused research and synthesis. Output: 2000-3500 words.

Phase 2 only begins after Phase 1 produces a GO recommendation. Never deep-dive into an audience for a market that has not been validated.
