# Launch Division -- Knowledge Base

> Strategic knowledge governing all launch operations. This document is loaded by all launch sub-agents as foundational context for planning, executing, and analyzing launches.

---

## Launch Philosophy

A launch is not a marketing event. It is the **coordinated deployment** of every upstream asset into a time-bound revenue window. The quality of a launch is determined before the cart opens -- by the depth of the pre-launch runway, the completeness of the asset inventory, and the precision of the cross-division coordination.

The difference between a $50K launch and a $500K launch is rarely the offer. It is the pre-launch runway and the daily execution discipline. Every launch that underperforms can trace its failure to one of three root causes:

1. **Insufficient runway** -- the audience was not warmed, beliefs were not installed, anticipation was not built
2. **Missing or broken assets** -- a funnel page that did not load, an email that was not sent, a payment link that failed
3. **Mismatched launch type** -- a Live Launch to a cold audience, an Evergreen funnel without paid traffic, a Flash Sale to an audience that has never been sold to

## The Five Launch Types

### 1. Live Launch

The highest-revenue, highest-coordination launch type. Creates a real event with real energy, real deadlines, and real scarcity.

**Best for:** Proven offers with engaged audiences of 1,000+ email subscribers or 5,000+ social followers.

**Timeline:** 4-6 weeks total (2-4 weeks pre-launch runway + 7-14 day open cart window).

**Revenue pattern:** 60-80% of total revenue arrives in the final 72 hours. This is normal, not alarming. The pre-launch and early window build the pressure; the close converts it.

**Key characteristics:**
- Real deadlines (cart closes, enrollment closes)
- Real scarcity (limited spots, bonus expiration)
- Omnipresent launch energy across all channels
- Creator is actively present -- going live, DMing, responding
- Emotional arc follows: curiosity > education > desire > urgency > decision

**Risk factors:** High coordination cost, creator energy drain, audience fatigue if repeated too frequently (minimum 8 weeks between Live Launches to the same audience).

### 2. Evergreen Launch

An automated funnel that mimics the urgency of a Live Launch but runs continuously, triggered by individual opt-ins.

**Best for:** Scalable offers with paid traffic budgets. The creator does not need to be actively present.

**Timeline:** 2-3 weeks to build and test, then always-on.

**Revenue pattern:** Steady daily conversions. No spikes, but consistent and predictable.

**Key characteristics:**
- Entry trigger: opt-in to lead magnet or webinar registration
- Deadline funnel: unique countdown per lead (48-72 hour window after trigger event)
- Automated email sequence: 5-7 emails over 5-7 days
- Pre-recorded webinar or VSL as the core conversion mechanism
- Retargeting ads for funnel visitors who did not convert

**Risk factors:** Requires ongoing paid traffic to fuel. Conversion rates decay over time as audiences saturate. Needs quarterly creative refresh.

### 3. Rolling Launch

Continuous enrollment with periodic cohort starts. Combines the always-on nature of Evergreen with the community energy of Live.

**Best for:** Cohort-based programs, group coaching, and memberships with structured onboarding.

**Timeline:** Continuous, with cohort start dates every 4-8 weeks.

**Revenue pattern:** Predictable monthly baseline with peaks around cohort close dates.

**Key characteristics:**
- Always accepting applications/registrations
- Cohort starts create natural urgency ("next cohort starts in X days")
- Content can reference current cohort results as social proof
- Onboarding is standardized and repeatable
- Each cohort close is a mini-launch event

**Risk factors:** Requires strong operational systems (SOPs, onboarding automation). Weaker urgency than Live Launch because "there is always a next cohort."

### 4. Flash Sale

A short, intense promotion designed for existing customers or warm audiences only. Price-driven, not value-driven.

**Best for:** Existing customer lists of 500+, seasonal moments, or clearing inventory of a product being retired.

**Timeline:** 1-2 weeks prep + 24-72 hour window.

**Revenue pattern:** Concentrated spike -- 90%+ of revenue within the window. Often the highest per-day revenue of any launch type.

**Key characteristics:**
- Significant discount or special offer (not a standard promotion)
- Limited to existing audience (no cold traffic)
- Simple messaging: here is the deal, here is the deadline
- Minimal asset production -- leverages existing sales pages and sequences
- Often used between major launches to maintain revenue momentum

**Risk factors:** Trains audience to wait for discounts if overused (maximum 2 per year). Attracts price-sensitive buyers with lower LTV. Should never be the primary launch strategy.

### 5. Beta Launch

Early access pricing with a feedback loop. The goal is learning and testimonial generation, not maximum revenue.

**Best for:** New offers that need market validation, creator's first offer, or significant offer redesigns.

**Timeline:** 2-3 week runway + 7 day window.

**Revenue pattern:** Modest -- pricing is below market to attract early adopters. Revenue is a secondary goal.

**Key characteristics:**
- Below-market pricing in exchange for feedback commitment
- Small cohort (10-25 people) for maximum learning
- Structured feedback collection at regular intervals
- Rapid iteration based on feedback
- Explicit testimonial collection built into the program
- Graduates become case studies and affiliates for the full launch

**Risk factors:** Low revenue per unit. Requires active feedback management. If not framed correctly, "beta" can imply the product is not ready.

## Cross-Division Coordination Model

A launch touches every division. The Launch Division does not produce these assets -- it requisitions them.

### Asset Dependency Map

```
FOUNDATIONS (must exist before any launch)
├── reference/icp.md -- Audience definition, pain points, beliefs
├── reference/offers.md -- Offer stack, pricing, guarantee
├── reference/positioning.md -- Mechanism, differentiation
└── reference/brand-voice.md -- Voice constraints for all copy

MARKETING DIVISION (content + ads)
├── Content calendar extract -- launch theme, daily posts, stories
├── Ad creatives -- launch-specific campaigns (retarget + cold)
├── Social proof assets -- testimonials, case studies, results graphics
└── SEO/blog content -- supporting articles if timeline allows

NURTURE DIVISION (email + SMS)
├── Launch email sequence -- type-specific (live vs evergreen vs flash)
├── SMS sequence -- cart open, mid-launch, final countdown
├── Webinar/challenge script -- if applicable to launch type
└── Retargeting sequences -- for non-converters post-launch

SALES DIVISION (conversion assets)
├── Sales page / funnel pages -- with launch-specific copy
├── VSL -- if applicable
├── Sales call scripts -- updated for launch offer
├── DM sales sequences -- personalized outreach templates
└── Objection handling scripts -- launch-specific objections

SCALE DIVISION (operations)
├── Launch execution SOP -- who does what, when, with what tool
├── Client onboarding SOP -- ready for new customer influx
└── Payment processing verification -- tested and confirmed
```

### Coordination Timeline

| Weeks Before Cart Open | Action | Division |
|------------------------|--------|----------|
| -6 | Launch type selected, revenue target set | Launch |
| -5 | Asset request briefs issued to all divisions | Launch |
| -4 | Sales page draft, email sequence draft, content calendar extract | Sales, Nurture, Marketing |
| -3 | Ad creatives produced, VSL recorded (if applicable) | Marketing, Sales |
| -2 | All assets in final review, automations built | All |
| -1 | Technical dry run, soft launch to VIP list | Launch |
| 0 | Cart open | Launch |

## Launch Economics

### Revenue Target Reverse-Engineering

Every launch plan begins by working backward from the revenue target:

```
Revenue Target: $100,000
Price Per Unit: $2,000
Units Needed: 50

Sales Page Conversion Rate (warm): 8%
Sales Page Visitors Needed: 625

Email Click-Through Rate: 5%
Emails Needed to Reach 625 Clicks: 12,500 sends (across sequence)

List Size Required: ~3,000 (assuming 4-5 emails during launch window)

If list < 3,000: Must supplement with paid traffic, DM outreach, or partner promotion
```

### Launch KPI Benchmarks

| KPI | Poor | Average | Good | Excellent |
|-----|------|---------|------|-----------|
| Waitlist-to-sale conversion | <3% | 3-8% | 8-15% | >15% |
| Email open rate (launch) | <20% | 20-30% | 30-40% | >40% |
| Email click rate (launch) | <2% | 2-4% | 4-7% | >7% |
| Sales page conversion (warm) | <3% | 3-8% | 8-15% | >15% |
| Sales page conversion (cold) | <0.5% | 0.5-2% | 2-5% | >5% |
| Webinar show rate | <20% | 20-35% | 35-50% | >50% |
| Webinar-to-sale conversion | <3% | 3-8% | 8-15% | >15% |
| Call show rate | <50% | 50-70% | 70-85% | >85% |
| Call close rate | <10% | 10-25% | 25-40% | >40% |
| Refund rate | >15% | 8-15% | 3-8% | <3% |
| ROAS (paid traffic) | <1.5x | 1.5-3x | 3-5x | >5x |

## Pre-Launch Belief Installation

Every launch must install specific beliefs before the cart opens. The pre-launch runway exists to do this work.

### Required Beliefs (Install in Order)

| Belief | Install Via | Timeline |
|--------|-----------|----------|
| 1. The problem is real and costly | Content: pain agitation posts, stories, data | Weeks -4 to -3 |
| 2. A solution exists | Content: mechanism introduction, educational content | Weeks -3 to -2 |
| 3. This mechanism is different | Content: why other approaches fail, mechanism demonstration | Weeks -2 to -1 |
| 4. This creator is credible | Content: social proof, case studies, credentials | Weeks -2 to -1 |
| 5. I can do this | Content: relatable case studies, "people like me" proof | Week -1 |
| 6. Now is the time | Content: urgency cues, opportunity cost, scarcity signals | Week -1 to Day 1 |

If any belief is missing from the pre-launch content plan, the launch will underperform. Belief 5 ("I can do this") is the most commonly skipped and the most important for high-ticket offers.

## Post-Launch Intelligence Cycle

Every launch generates data that improves the next launch. The post-launch analysis feeds directly back into launch planning.

### Data Flow

```
Launch Execution
    │
    ▼
Raw Data Collection (during launch)
    │ Email metrics, ad metrics, funnel metrics, DM conversations
    │ Sales call recordings, refund reasons, survey responses
    │
    ▼
Post-Launch Analysis (within 5 business days)
    │ Funnel diagnostics, channel ROI, customer profile analysis
    │ Revenue vs target gap analysis, recommendation prioritization
    │
    ▼
Launch Learnings Archive
    │ What worked, what did not, what to test next
    │
    ▼
Next Launch Plan (incorporates learnings)
```

### Continuous Improvement Metrics

Track these across launches to measure operational improvement:

| Metric | Direction | Indicates |
|--------|-----------|-----------|
| Revenue per launch | Increasing | Audience growth + offer maturation |
| Conversion rate trend | Increasing | Better messaging + better targeting |
| CAC per launch | Decreasing | More efficient acquisition |
| Refund rate trend | Decreasing | Better audience-offer fit |
| Asset readiness at runway start | 100% | Operational maturity |
| Time from cart close to report | Decreasing | Analytical speed |

## Launch Calendar Management

### Spacing Rules

| Launch Type | Minimum Spacing (Same Audience) | Rationale |
|-------------|-------------------------------|-----------|
| Live Launch | 8 weeks | Audience fatigue prevention |
| Evergreen | Always-on (no spacing needed) | Automated, individual timing |
| Rolling | 4-8 weeks between cohort closes | Maintains urgency per cohort |
| Flash Sale | 16 weeks (max 2/year) | Prevents discount expectation |
| Beta Launch | No restriction | Different audience segment |

### Seasonal Considerations

| Quarter | Launch Considerations |
|---------|----------------------|
| Q1 (Jan-Mar) | New Year energy peaks in January, drops by mid-February. Best for transformation-focused offers. |
| Q2 (Apr-Jun) | Steady engagement. Good for mid-tier launches. Avoid late June (summer drop-off). |
| Q3 (Jul-Sep) | Summer lull July-August. September is a strong reactivation month. |
| Q4 (Oct-Dec) | Peak buying season. Black Friday/Cyber Monday for Flash Sales. December is difficult (holiday distraction). |
