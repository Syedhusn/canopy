## Team Management Principles

### The Creator Business Team Evolution

Creator businesses evolve through predictable team stages. Hiring out of sequence causes the same failures every time.

**Stage 1: Solo ($0-$10K/mo)**
The creator does everything. No team needed yet, but this is when SOPs should start being written -- document what you do while you do it, because you will forget the details once you delegate.

**Stage 2: First Leverage ($10K-$30K/mo)**
The first hire should eliminate the creator's biggest non-revenue time sink. Usually a VA/Admin ($1.5K-$3K/mo) handling scheduling, inbox, and administrative tasks. The second hire should directly increase revenue capacity -- usually a Setter ($2K-$4K/mo) to handle inbound lead qualification.

**Stage 3: Revenue Team ($30K-$100K/mo)**
The sales function scales: Closer joins the team. Content production becomes a bottleneck, so Content Manager and/or Video Editor join. If client base exceeds 20, Customer Success becomes critical to protect recurring revenue.

**Stage 4: Management Layer ($100K+/mo)**
Individual contributors need management. Marketing Manager owns campaign execution. Operations Manager coordinates across functions. The creator shifts from doing to directing.

### Hiring Decision Framework

Before hiring any role, answer these five questions:

1. **Is there an SOP for this role's core process?** If no, write the SOP first.
2. **Can you afford this hire for 6 months even if they produce no revenue?** If no, wait.
3. **Will this hire directly increase revenue or free 10+ hours/week of creator time?** If neither, deprioritize.
4. **Can you train this person in under 30 days using your existing SOPs?** If no, improve your SOPs first.
5. **Do you have the bandwidth to manage this person for the first 90 days?** If no, hire a VA first to free management time.

### Compensation Philosophy

Creator business compensation follows three principles:

**Principle 1: Align incentives with outcomes.** Sales roles earn based on revenue generated. Retention roles earn based on churn reduction. Content roles earn based on production quality and consistency.

**Principle 2: Keep base salaries survivable but not comfortable.** The base should cover living expenses without financial stress, but the upside from variable compensation should be the primary motivator for revenue-impacting roles.

**Principle 3: Pay at or above market to reduce turnover cost.** The cost of rehiring (recruiting, training, lost productivity during ramp) typically equals 3-6 months of salary. Paying 10-20% above market to retain good people is almost always net positive.

### Performance Management Cadence

| Period | Review Type | Focus |
|--------|-----------|-------|
| Weekly | 1-on-1 (30 min) | Priorities, blockers, quick feedback |
| Monthly | Performance check | KPI review against targets |
| Quarterly | Deep review | Goal setting, development plan, compensation review |
| Annually | Full evaluation | Retention decision, role evolution, promotion assessment |

### Common Management Mistakes in Creator Businesses

1. **Hiring friends or fans instead of qualified candidates.** Loyalty does not equal competence. Use the hiring brief and scoring rubric without exception.

2. **No clear KPIs.** "Do a great job" is not measurable. Every role needs 3-5 specific, quantifiable metrics that define success.

3. **Micromanaging instead of managing by metrics.** If the SOP is good and the KPIs are clear, check the numbers, not the process. Intervention is needed only when metrics are off target.

4. **Slow to fire.** Creator businesses cannot afford passengers. The "enthusiastic rehire" test at 90 days is binary: if you would not enthusiastically rehire this person knowing what you know now, make the change.

5. **No onboarding structure.** Handing someone access and saying "figure it out" produces 90-day failures. The 30/60/90 plan is not optional.

## SOP Methodology Deep Dive

### Why SOPs Fail

The two most common SOP failures, in order:

1. **Over-documentation (50+ page manuals nobody reads).** The fix: no single SOP exceeds 3 pages. Break complex processes into sub-procedures that reference each other.

2. **Under-documentation (vague steps that require interpretation).** The fix: the "new hire test." Have someone who has never done the task follow the SOP. Every question they ask reveals an ambiguity to fix.

### The SOP Writing Process

**Step 1: Process Discovery**
Do not write an SOP from memory. Watch the process happen in real time (screen recording or shadowing). Document every click, decision, and handoff. Pay special attention to moments where the person "uses judgment" -- those are the decision trees you need to build.

**Step 2: Draft in Template**
Use the standard SOP template: Purpose > Scope > Prerequisites > Procedure > Decision Points > Quality Checks > Escalation Triggers > Common Mistakes > KPIs > Change Log.

**Step 3: The New Hire Test**
Have someone unfamiliar with the task follow the SOP exactly. Document every question asked. Each question is an ambiguity that needs to be resolved in the SOP.

**Step 4: Time Calibration**
Time each step during the new hire test. Update estimated times to match reality. This data becomes the basis for workload planning.

**Step 5: Deploy and Version**
Assign an owner. Set a review cadence (monthly for high-change processes, quarterly for stable ones). Log every change in the change log with date, description, and author.

### Decision Tree Design Principles

Decision trees are the backbone of good SOPs. They turn "judgment calls" into documented if/then branches.

**Format:**
```
[Step N] Check if [observable condition]
├── YES → [Specific action or proceed to Step N+1]
└── NO → Check if [sub-condition]
    ├── [Sub-condition A] → [Action A]
    ├── [Sub-condition B] → [Action B]
    └── [None of the above] → ESCALATE to [role] via [channel]
```

**Rules for good decision trees:**
- Every condition must be observable (not subjective). "The lead seems interested" is bad. "The lead asked about pricing" is good.
- Every branch must end in a specific action or an explicit escalation.
- The "none of the above" branch always escalates -- never leave unhandled cases.
- Maximum 3 levels of nesting. If deeper, break into a sub-procedure.

### Tool-Specific Guidance

SOPs must specify exact tools and navigation paths. The difference between a useful SOP and a useless one is often this level of detail.

**Good:** "Open GoHighLevel > Contacts > Smart List: 'New Leads Today' > Click first lead > Review the Notes tab"

**Bad:** "Check new leads in the CRM"

Include screenshots or screen recordings for complex tool workflows. Link them inline in the SOP rather than embedding (keeps file size manageable and allows easy updates).

## Case Study Production Knowledge

### Why Case Studies Matter More Than Any Other Content Type

In creator/coaching businesses, case studies answer the single most important question a prospect has: "Will this work for someone like me?" No amount of educational content, testimonials, or authority positioning answers this question as powerfully as a detailed story of someone similar who got the result.

### The VOC (Voice of Customer) Principle

The most powerful case studies use the client's own words, not polished copywriting. When a prospect reads "I was skeptical because I'd tried three other programs" in the client's genuine voice, it resonates more deeply than any professionally crafted sentence. The case study writer's job is to structure the narrative and extract the best moments -- not to rewrite the client's experience in marketing language.

### Case Study Distribution Strategy

A single case study produces multiple assets:

| Asset | Derived From | Used By |
|-------|-------------|---------|
| Full written case study (800-1500 words) | Interview + data | Sales page, email sequence |
| Pull quotes (2-3 per study) | Key moments from interview | Social posts, ad creative, email signatures |
| Before/after graphic | Quantified start and end state | Social posts, ads, VSL slides |
| Video testimonial clip (30-90 seconds) | Video interview excerpt | Social reels, ads, VSL B-roll |
| Results timeline | Milestone data | Sales page, webinar slides |
| Social proof snippet (1-2 sentences) | Best single quote | Everywhere -- email, social, ads, funnel pages |

### Interview Timing

The best time to conduct a case study interview is 2-4 weeks after the client achieves their primary result. Too early and the results are not yet concrete. Too late and the emotional memory of the transformation has faded, producing less vivid and less authentic responses.

### Legal and Ethical Considerations

- Always get written permission before publishing any client's name, photo, or results
- Include income/results disclaimers where legally required
- Never pressure a client to provide a testimonial -- it must be genuine
- If a client's results are atypical, note this in the case study
- Respect client privacy: some clients want their story shared but with a changed name or without their photo. Accommodate this.

## Sales Team Specifics

### Setter Economics

The setter role is typically the first sales hire and has specific economics:

```
Setter Cost: $3,000/month base + $100/qualified booking
Average Bookings/Month: 60
Total Setter Cost: $3,000 + $6,000 = $9,000/month

Closer converts 30% of bookings: 18 sales
Average sale: $3,000
Monthly revenue from setter's pipeline: $54,000

ROI on setter: $54,000 / $9,000 = 6:1

Break-even: Setter needs to produce just 3 closed sales/month to cover their cost
```

### The 5-Minute Rule

Research consistently shows that responding to an inbound lead within 5 minutes increases qualification rates by 400% compared to a 30-minute response. The setter's #1 KPI is response time.

### Commission Structure Best Practices

| Role | Base | Variable | Why This Structure |
|------|------|----------|-------------------|
| Setter | 40-60% of OTE | 40-60% per-booking bonus | Incentivizes volume of qualified bookings |
| Closer | 30-40% of OTE | 60-70% commission on closed revenue | Incentivizes closing ability and deal size |
| SDR | 50-60% of OTE | 40-50% per-qualified-lead bonus | Incentivizes outbound volume with quality filter |

### Follow-Up Timing Protocol

| Touchpoint | Timing | Channel | Purpose |
|-----------|--------|---------|---------|
| First contact | Within 5 minutes of lead action | Phone/SMS | Capitalize on peak interest |
| If no answer | 30 minutes later | SMS | "Hey, tried calling. When's a good time?" |
| If no response | 4 hours later | Email | Value-add email with relevant content |
| If no response | Next business day, 10am | Phone call | Second attempt |
| If no response | Day 3 | SMS | "Last try -- still interested in [topic]?" |
| If no response | Day 5 | Move to nurture | Remove from active outreach, enter email nurture |
