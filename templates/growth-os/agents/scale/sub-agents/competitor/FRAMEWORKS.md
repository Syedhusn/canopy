# Competitive Intelligence Frameworks

> Complete frameworks for battlecard production, SWOT analysis, market positioning analysis, and ongoing competitive monitoring. Each framework includes templates, methodology, and actionable output formats.

---

## Framework 1: Competitive Battlecard

### Purpose

A one-page sales enablement document per major competitor that arms the sales team with specific talk tracks for handling competitor objections during sales calls and DM conversations. Battlecards are the most operationally valuable competitive intelligence output.

```markdown
## Objection Handling Talk Tracks

### "I'm also looking at [Competitor]"
**Response:** "That's smart to compare options. Can I ask what specifically appeals to you about [Competitor]? ... [listen] ... Here's how we approach that differently: [specific differentiation]. The key difference is [mechanism differentiation]. Would it help if I shared a case study of someone who was in a similar situation?"

### "[Competitor] is cheaper"
**Response:** "You're right, they are. And here's why: [explain what is not included in their offer that is in ours]. When you factor in [specific value component], the actual cost-per-result is [our advantage]. Let me show you the math..."

### "[Competitor] has more testimonials / bigger audience"
**Response:** "They do have a larger audience, and they've been at this longer. What I'd encourage you to look at is not the volume of testimonials but the specificity. Look at [our case study] -- [specific client, specific result, specific timeline]. That level of documented transformation is what matters. Also, larger programs often mean less personal attention -- here's what your experience looks like with us: [personalization details]."

### "My friend recommended [Competitor]"
**Response:** "Referrals are powerful -- your friend clearly had a good experience. Let me ask: does your friend's situation match yours? [probe for specifics] ... The reason I ask is that [Competitor] tends to work best for [their ideal client type], while our approach is specifically designed for [our ICP description]. Let me share a case study of someone who matches your exact situation..."

---

## The Killer Question
The one question that exposes their biggest weakness:
> "[Question that, when the prospect investigates, reveals the competitor's gap]"

Example: "Ask them specifically what their average client's results are at 90 days -- not their best case study, but their average."

---

## Intelligence Notes
- [Date]: [Observation about recent change, pricing update, launch activity]
- [Date]: [Another observation]
```

### Battlecard Maintenance Protocol

| Trigger | Action | Timeline |
|---------|--------|----------|
| Competitor changes pricing | Update battlecard pricing section and talk tracks | Within 7 days |
| Competitor launches new offer | Update "At a Glance" and head-to-head comparison | Within 7 days |
| Competitor receives notable negative review | Add to Weaknesses with source and date | Within 3 days |
| New case study available that counters competitor | Add to talk tracks with specific reference | Within 3 days |
| Quarterly review | Full battlecard audit for accuracy and currency | Every 90 days |

## Framework 2: SWOT Analysis

### Purpose

Structured assessment of each competitor's Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, and Threats to identify strategic advantages and vulnerability windows.

### SWOT Template

```markdown
# SWOT Analysis: [Competitor Name]
**Analysis Date:** [date]
**Analyst Confidence:** [High / Medium / Low]

## Strengths (Internal Positive)
What they do well that we should respect and not try to compete head-on:
1. [Strength] -- Evidence: [source, date]
   - Strategic implication: [what this means for us]
2. [Strength] -- Evidence: [source, date]
   - Strategic implication: [what this means for us]
3. [Strength]

## Weaknesses (Internal Negative)
Where they fall short that we can exploit:
1. [Weakness] -- Evidence: [source, date]
   - Exploitation strategy: [how we can capitalize]
2. [Weakness] -- Evidence: [source, date]
   - Exploitation strategy: [how we can capitalize]
3. [Weakness]

## Opportunities (External Positive)
Market conditions or competitor actions that create openings for us:
1. [Opportunity] -- Evidence: [what we observed]
   - Recommended action: [specific step]
   - Timing: [when to act]
2. [Opportunity]
3. [Opportunity]

## Threats (External Negative)
Market conditions or competitor actions that could hurt us:
1. [Threat] -- Evidence: [what we observed]
   - Mitigation: [how to defend]
   - Monitoring: [what to watch for]
2. [Threat]
3. [Threat]

## Net Assessment
**Overall competitive position:** [Strong / Neutral / Vulnerable]
**Biggest strategic advantage:** [one sentence]
**Biggest strategic risk:** [one sentence]
**Recommended priority action:** [one specific action]
```

### SWOT Analysis Best Practices

1. **Evidence over opinion.** Every SWOT entry must cite an observable data point. "They seem to be struggling" is not evidence. "Their average YouTube engagement dropped 40% over the last 6 months based on Social Blade data" is evidence.

2. **Strengths must be genuine.** Dismissing competitor strengths produces blind spots. If they have better social proof, acknowledge it and build a strategy around it.

3. **Weaknesses must be exploitable.** A weakness that cannot be exploited is strategically irrelevant. "Their website is ugly" is only a weakness if it measurably hurts their conversion -- and even then, only if we can demonstrate a superior experience.

4. **Opportunities have expiration dates.** A vulnerability window after a competitor's failed launch closes within 4-6 weeks. Document timing alongside the opportunity.

5. **Threats require monitoring triggers.** Do not just identify threats -- define what signal would indicate the threat is materializing, and set up monitoring.

## Framework 3: Market Positioning Analysis

### Purpose

Map the competitive landscape visually and strategically to identify positioning white space, crowded quadrants, and the creator's optimal position.

### The Positioning Matrix (2x2)

```
                    HIGH PRICE
                        │
                        │
    Premium             │           Premium
    Generalist          │           Specialist
    (overcrowded)       │           (high-value white space)
                        │
   ─────────────────────┼────────────────────
                        │
    Budget              │           Budget
    Generalist          │           Specialist
    (race to bottom)    │           (niche opportunity)
                        │
                        │
                    LOW PRICE

    GENERALIST ─────────┼──────── SPECIALIST
```

### Positioning Map Template

```markdown
# Market Positioning Map: [Market/Niche]
**Date:** [date]
**Competitors Mapped:** [number]

## Quadrant Analysis

### Premium Specialist (Target Quadrant for Most Creator Businesses)
**Who's here:** [list competitors in this quadrant]
**Saturation:** [Low / Medium / High]
**White space:** [specific positioning angles not yet claimed]

### Premium Generalist
**Who's here:** [list]
**Risk:** This quadrant invites "but are they really the best for ME?" objections
**Note:** [observations]

### Budget Specialist
**Who's here:** [list]
**Opportunity:** [if any]
**Warning:** Competing here requires volume-based economics

### Budget Generalist
**Who's here:** [list]
**Assessment:** Avoid -- race to the bottom

## Creator's Current Position
**Quadrant:** [which quadrant]
**Nearest competitors:** [who is closest in positioning]
**Differentiation gap:** [how much distance between us and nearest competitor]

## Recommended Position
**Target quadrant:** [where to position]
**Positioning angle:** [specific differentiation]
**Required proof:** [what evidence is needed to claim this position]
```

## Framework 4: Win/Loss Analysis

### Purpose

Systematically analyze why deals were won or lost against specific competitors to improve competitive positioning and sales effectiveness.

### Data Collection Template

For every sales conversation where a competitor is mentioned:

```markdown
## Win/Loss Record: [Prospect Name/ID]
**Date:** [date]
**Outcome:** [Won / Lost]
**Competitor:** [competitor name]
**Deal Value:** $[amount]

### Context
- How did the prospect find us? [source]
- Were they already talking to the competitor? [yes/no/unknown]
- At what stage did the competitor come up? [discovery / pitch / negotiation]

### Why We Won (if won)
- Primary reason: [specific factor]
- Secondary reasons: [list]
- Prospect's own words: "[verbatim quote about why they chose us]"

### Why We Lost (if lost)
- Primary reason: [specific factor -- price / features / trust / timing / fit]
- Secondary reasons: [list]
- Prospect's own words: "[verbatim quote about why they chose competitor]"
- Could we have won? [yes / no / maybe]
- What would have needed to change? [specific action]

### Lessons for Competitive Strategy
- [What this tells us about our positioning vs this competitor]
```

## Framework 5: Vulnerability Window Detection

### Purpose

Identify time-limited competitive opportunities -- moments when a competitor is weak and the creator can compete most effectively.

### Vulnerability Types and Indicators

| Window Type | Detection Signals | Recommended Response | Urgency |
|-------------|------------------|---------------------|---------|
| Post-launch fatigue | Competitor just closed a launch; their audience is saturated and their content quality dips | Launch competing offer to capture dissatisfied non-buyers; increase content targeting their audience keywords | Act within 2-4 weeks |
| Negative sentiment surge | Multiple negative reviews, refund complaints on social, public disputes | Create content addressing the pain points the competitor caused; position as the "safe alternative" | Act within 1-2 weeks |
| Pricing gap | Competitor raised prices significantly without visible value add | Position as the accessible premium option; emphasize value-for-money comparison | Act within 4 weeks |
| Content gap | Competitor abandons a platform or reduces content frequency | Dominate the platform they left; capture their audience | Ongoing opportunity |
| Team transition | Key team member departure visible (LinkedIn, social announcements) | Expect temporary quality decline in their delivery; compete on consistency and reliability | 4-8 week window |
| Offer stagnation | Same offer, same messaging for 6+ months with no visible innovation | Position your innovation and freshness; highlight "the market has changed since they built their program" | Ongoing |
| Technology failure | Competitor's funnel, website, or platform has visible issues | Ensure your own systems are flawless; capture redirected traffic if possible | Immediate but brief |

### Monitoring Protocol

| Source | What to Watch | Frequency | Alert Trigger |
|--------|-------------|-----------|--------------|
| Competitor social media | Engagement decline, negative comments, content gaps | Weekly | 30%+ engagement drop or 3+ negative comments in a week |
| Review platforms | New negative reviews, declining ratings | Bi-weekly | Any 1-star review with specific complaint |
| Ad libraries | Ad pause, creative staleness, new campaign launch | Weekly | All ads paused or no new creatives in 30 days |
| Email subscription | Launch sequence detected, pricing change, messaging shift | Ongoing | Any pricing or offer change |
| Job boards | New postings, posting removals | Monthly | Key role posting suggests strategic shift |
| Google Alerts | Brand name mentions, industry news | Daily | Negative press or controversy |
