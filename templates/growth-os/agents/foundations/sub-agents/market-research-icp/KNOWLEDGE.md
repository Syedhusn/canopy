## Info Product Market Patterns

### Market Size Heuristics

The info/education creator economy follows predictable size patterns:

| Market Tier | Annual Addressable Market | Typical Creator Revenue Ceiling | Examples |
|-------------|--------------------------|-------------------------------|----------|
| Macro Market | $1B+ | No practical ceiling | "Business," "Health," "Relationships" |
| Sub-Market | $100M-$1B | $10M+ with team | "Online business," "Weight loss," "Dating" |
| Niche | $10M-$100M | $1M-$10M solo or small team | "Agency growth," "Postpartum fitness," "Dating for introverts" |
| Micro-Niche | $1M-$10M | $100K-$1M solo | "Shopify agency scaling," "Diastasis recti recovery," "Dating for engineers" |

### Optimal Niche Characteristics for Info Creators

A niche is optimal for an info/education creator when it exhibits ALL of these signals:

1. **Recurring pain** -- the problem comes back or has phases (not a one-time fix)
2. **Identity attachment** -- the audience sees the problem as part of who they are, not just something they deal with
3. **Willingness to pay for speed** -- the free information exists but is overwhelming; they want a shortcut
4. **Community potential** -- people in this niche want to connect with others like them
5. **Transformation is visible** -- before/after states are demonstrable (not abstract)
6. **Creator has credibility** -- the creator has personal experience, results, or credentials the audience respects

## Red Flags in Market Research

These patterns indicate a niche should receive a Conditional or No-Go recommendation:

### Demand Red Flags
- **Declining search volume** over 24+ months with no recovery signal
- **Zero active advertisers** in Meta Ad Library (if no one is spending money to reach this audience, there may be no monetizable demand)
- **Content consumption without buying** -- high view counts on free content but no evidence of paid purchases (engagement without intent)
- **Seasonal-only demand** with no off-season baseline (risky for sustained business)

### Pain Red Flags
- **Nice-to-have pain only** -- the problem is annoying but not urgent enough to drive purchase behavior
- **One-time resolution** -- the pain resolves permanently after one solution (no recurring revenue potential)
- **Pain without purchasing power** -- the audience has intense pain but no money to solve it (common in student/youth niches)
- **Diffuse pain** -- the problem is too broad for the audience to self-identify ("I want to be happier" vs. "I need to stop anxiety attacks at work")

### Competition Red Flags
- **Single dominant player** with 70%+ market share and deep moats (brand, network effects, content library)
- **Race to zero** -- competitors aggressively undercutting each other on price with no differentiation
- **Copycat saturation** -- 10+ competitors with near-identical positioning, offers, and messaging
- **Well-funded entrants** -- VC-backed competitors with massive ad budgets entering the space

### Economics Red Flags
- **Price ceiling below $500** with no evidence of premium willingness -- limits LTV and makes CAC ratios very tight
- **High refund rates** across the market (15%+) -- indicates systemic delivery or expectation problems
- **Long sales cycles** (6+ months) without high ticket prices to justify the acquisition cost
- **Platform dependency** -- the audience is locked into a platform (Udemy, Skillshare) that controls pricing and discovery

## Green Lights in Market Research

These patterns indicate strong opportunity:

### Strong Go Signals
- **Active advertising** with multiple advertisers running ads for 3+ months (longevity = profitability)
- **Premium pricing evidence** -- $2,000+ offers actively selling in the space
- **Growing search volume** with 20%+ year-over-year growth in core terms
- **Community activity** -- active subreddits, Facebook groups, or Discord servers with high engagement
- **Failed attempt patterns** -- the audience has tried and failed with existing solutions (indicates need for a new mechanism)
- **Emerging subtopic** within a proven macro market -- the macro is validated but the specific angle is underserved

### ICP Readiness Signals
- **Verbatim language is abundant** -- forums and comments are rich with emotional, specific language about the problem
- **Clear self-identification** -- the audience describes themselves with specific labels ("I'm a struggling agency owner," "I'm a postpartum mom trying to...")
- **Active community participation** -- they ask questions, share stories, and engage deeply (not just lurking)
- **Purchase history visible** -- they talk about what they have bought before and why it did or did not work

## ICP Construction Heuristics

### The "2 AM Test"

A complete ICP should answer: "What does this person whisper to themselves at 2 AM when they cannot sleep?" If the ICP document cannot answer this with a specific, emotionally charged sentence drawn from real language data, the Psychographics and Pain Points sections are incomplete.

### Language Pattern Signals

When extracting verbatim language, prioritize phrases that contain:
- **Absolutes** -- "always," "never," "every time," "nothing works" (indicates emotional intensity)
- **Self-reference** -- "I feel," "I am," "I can't" (indicates identity attachment)
- **Time markers** -- "for years," "I've been struggling since," "still can't" (indicates chronic pain)
- **Comparison** -- "everyone else seems to," "other people can," "why can't I" (indicates Worthless belief)
- **Overwhelm markers** -- "too much," "don't know where to start," "information overload" (indicates Helpless belief)
- **Cynicism markers** -- "tried everything," "nothing works for me," "my situation is different" (indicates Hopeless belief)

### Common ICP Mistakes to Avoid

1. **The Demographic Trap** -- Defining the ICP purely by age, gender, and income without psychographic depth. A 35-year-old male making $80K who wants to grow his agency is not an ICP. What does he fear? What has he tried? What does he believe about himself?

2. **The Aspiration Trap** -- Describing only what the ICP wants without mapping what they have tried and failed. The "What They've Tried & Failed" section is what makes the ICP actionable for positioning.

3. **The Sanitization Trap** -- Cleaning up the ICP's language into polished marketing-speak. The raw, unfiltered, sometimes grammatically incorrect language is the gold. "I'm literally drowning in information and every course is the same bs" is more valuable than "They feel overwhelmed by available information."

4. **The Monolith Trap** -- Treating the ICP as one homogeneous person when there are distinct sub-segments. If the data shows two clearly different pain profiles within the same niche, document both and recommend the creator choose one. An ICP that tries to be everyone is no one.

5. **The Survey Trap** -- Over-relying on survey data (Rank 6 evidence) for pain points and willingness to pay. People lie in surveys. They say they would pay $2,000 for a course, then balk at $497. Always validate survey findings against behavioral evidence (Ranks 1-4).

## Competitive Analysis Patterns

### Competitor Positioning Archetypes

Most info product competitors fall into one of these positioning patterns:

| Archetype | Description | Vulnerability |
|-----------|-------------|---------------|
| **The Authority** | "I've done it, I'll show you how" | Vulnerable to process-focused competitors who make it accessible |
| **The System** | "Follow my proven 7-step system" | Vulnerable to personalization-focused competitors |
| **The Community** | "Join a group of people like you" | Vulnerable to results-focused competitors with guarantees |
| **The Shortcut** | "Skip the learning curve" | Vulnerable to depth-focused competitors who question shortcut sustainability |
| **The Contrarian** | "Everything you've been told is wrong" | Vulnerable to evidence-focused competitors who show nuance |

Understanding which archetype each competitor uses reveals their blind spots and your whitespace.

### Gap Analysis Method

For each competitor, document:
1. **Who they serve well** -- their ideal buyer, at their best
2. **Who they underserve** -- the segment that buys but is disappointed
3. **Who they ignore** -- the segment that has the pain but is not targeted
4. **What they promise vs. what they deliver** -- the gap between marketing and results
5. **Where their reviews are negative** -- specific complaints from 1-3 star reviews

The underserved and ignored segments are your opportunity. The promise-delivery gap is your mechanism design fuel.
