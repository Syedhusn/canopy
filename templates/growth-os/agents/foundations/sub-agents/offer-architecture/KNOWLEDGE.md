## Pricing Psychology for Info Products

### The Three Pricing Zones

Every info product market has three pricing zones. Each zone has different buyer psychology, proof requirements, and margin characteristics.

| Zone | Price Range | Buyer Psychology | Proof Required | Margin Profile |
|------|-----------|-----------------|---------------|----------------|
| **Impulse Zone** | $7-$97 | "Low risk, let me try it." Decision made in minutes. | Social proof (reviews, ratings). Minimal. | Low margin per unit. Volume-dependent. High refund tolerance. |
| **Consideration Zone** | $297-$2,997 | "I need to think about this." Decision made in days. | Case studies, testimonials, guarantee, mechanism explanation. | Moderate margin. Mix of volume and value. |
| **Investment Zone** | $3,000-$50,000+ | "This is a significant commitment." Decision made in weeks. | Deep proof: results, credentials, personal fit, ROI calculation, guarantee, conversation/call. | High margin per unit. Low volume required. Relationship-dependent. |

### Price Anchoring Methods

Price should never be presented in isolation. Always use at least two of these anchoring methods:

1. **Inaction Cost Anchor:** "If this problem costs you $X per month in lost [revenue/time/health], then in 12 months you'll have spent $12X doing nothing. This investment is [fraction] of that cost."

2. **Comparison Stack Anchor:** "A private consultant charges $X per hour. A mastermind costs $Y per year. Figuring this out alone costs $Z in mistakes. This program delivers more than all three combined for [fraction] of the total."

3. **ROI Timeline Anchor:** "If this produces [result], that's worth $X per [period]. Your investment pays for itself in [timeframe], and every month after that is pure upside."

4. **Component Value Anchor:** "Module 1 alone is worth $X (here's why). Module 2 is worth $Y. The templates save you $Z in time. Total value: $V. Your investment: $P."

### Pricing Mistakes in Info Products

| Mistake | Why It Happens | Consequence |
|---------|---------------|-------------|
| Pricing too low | Creator lacks confidence or fears rejection | Attracts low-commitment buyers, devalues the transformation, unsustainable economics |
| Pricing too high without proof | Creator heard "charge premium" but has no results to justify it | Slow sales, high refund rates, reputation damage |
| No tiers | Creator offers one price point | Leaves money on the table (some would pay more) and loses price-sensitive buyers |
| Pricing based on content volume | "It has 47 videos so it must be worth $997" | Buyers do not pay for content -- they pay for transformation. Volume can actually REDUCE perceived value (more effort = lower PV score) |
| Frequent discounting | Creator uses discounts to drive urgency | Trains the audience to wait for sales. Devalues the brand. Attracts discount-seekers, not transformation-seekers. |

## Value Stacking Strategy

### The Stack Structure

A well-designed value stack follows a specific order of presentation. Each layer serves a strategic purpose.

| Layer | Purpose | Design Principle |
|-------|---------|-----------------|
| **Core Offer** | The main transformation. The thing they are actually buying. | Must be a complete solution on its own. If the bonuses disappeared, the core should still justify the price. |
| **Speed Bonus** | Accelerates results. Gets them to the first win faster. | Templates, checklists, quick-start guides, 72-hour action plans. Reduces Time Delay in the Value Equation. |
| **Friction Bonus** | Removes an obstacle that would slow them down. | Done-for-you elements, tech setup guides, scripts, swipe files. Reduces Effort in the Value Equation. |
| **Status Bonus** | Adds exclusivity, community, or access. | Private community, VIP group, expert Q&A access, networking events. Increases Perceived Likelihood through social proof. |
| **Objection Bonus** | Directly addresses the #1 objection from the ICP. | Guarantee enhancement, "what if it doesn't work" module, personalized assessment. Increases Perceived Likelihood. |
| **Fast-Action Bonus** | Rewards decisive buyers. Creates urgency. | Extra coaching call, bonus module, priority onboarding. Expires within 24-72 hours. |

### Perceived Value Rules

1. **Assign a specific dollar value to each bonus.** Not arbitrary -- based on what the component would cost if purchased separately.
2. **Total perceived value must be >= 10x the price.** A $3,000 offer should have $30,000+ in perceived value. This is achievable with well-designed bonuses.
3. **Never add bonuses just to increase the count.** Each bonus must serve a specific strategic purpose (speed, friction, status, objection, urgency).
4. **Bonuses should have near-zero marginal cost.** Digital assets, templates, recorded trainings, community access. If a bonus costs significant money per customer, it is not a bonus -- it is a core deliverable that should be priced accordingly.

## Risk Reversal Psychology

### Why Guarantees Work

The guarantee is the single highest-leverage element in most offers. It works because:

1. **It shifts risk from buyer to creator.** The buyer's calculation changes from "I might lose $3,000" to "the worst case is I get my money back."
2. **It signals confidence.** A bold guarantee communicates "I believe in this so strongly that I'm willing to take the risk myself."
3. **It addresses the Hopeless belief directly.** For buyers who believe "nothing works for me," a guarantee is the only thing that makes trying again rational.

### Guarantee Economics

Counter-intuitively, bold guarantees typically REDUCE refund rates rather than increasing them. This is because:
- **Selection effect:** A guarantee attracts more serious buyers (they would not commit even with a guarantee if they were not serious)
- **Commitment effect:** Once the buyer has started the program, they are psychologically invested and less likely to request a refund
- **Completion effect:** Conditional guarantees that require completing specific actions filter out non-committed buyers AND increase engagement (which improves results)

**Industry benchmarks for refund rates:**
- No guarantee: 8-15% refund rate (higher because buyers have no recourse, so they buy impulsively and regret)
- Generic money-back: 5-10% (lower because buyers feel safer and make better decisions)
- Specific conditional guarantee: 3-8% (lowest because conditions create commitment filter)

## Info Product Economics

### Unit Economics Model

The standard info product business has these economics:

```
Revenue per customer = Tier Price - (Refund Rate x Tier Price)
Gross profit = Revenue - COGS (platform fees, payment processing, delivery costs)
Net revenue = Gross profit - CAC
```

For digital products, COGS is typically 5-15% (payment processing 2.9%, platform fees 2-10%, delivery costs negligible).

### LTV Calculation for Tiered Offers

```
Blended LTV = (% buying Downsell x Downsell Price) +
              (% buying Flagship x Flagship Price) +
              (% buying Upsell x Upsell Price) +
              (Upsell Rate x Upsell Price from any tier)

Adjusted for refunds:
Net LTV = Blended LTV x (1 - Refund Rate)
```

**Typical distribution across tiers:**
- Downsell: 30-40% of buyers
- Flagship: 45-55% of buyers
- Upsell: 10-20% of buyers

### Break-Even Analysis

```
Break-Even Units = Fixed Costs / (Average Revenue per Customer - Variable Cost per Customer)

Fixed Costs include:
- Content production (one-time)
- Platform subscriptions (monthly)
- Team costs (if any)
- Marketing infrastructure (email, landing pages)

Variable Costs include:
- Payment processing per transaction
- Coaching time per customer (if included)
- Support costs per customer
```

### Common Economic Failure Modes

| Failure Mode | Cause | Fix |
|-------------|-------|-----|
| CAC > Price | Targeting wrong audience or wrong awareness level | Warm traffic first. Build audience before selling. Use content to reduce CAC. |
| Low LTV, high CAC | No ascension path, single tier offer | Add tiers. Design upsell. Build retention loop (community, recurring). |
| High refund rate | Offer-market mismatch, wrong expectations set, delivery gap | Audit ICP alignment. Fix sales messaging. Improve onboarding. Add momentum loop. |
| Slow payback | Long sales cycle without high enough price | Shorter funnel for lower tiers. Webinar/challenge model for faster conversion. |

## Positioning Strategy Knowledge

### Category Creation vs. Category Competition

| Approach | When to Use | How It Works | Risk |
|----------|------------|-------------|------|
| **Category Competition** | Stage 1-2 markets with few competitors | Position as the best option in an existing category | Low risk but limited ceiling. Vulnerable to better-funded competitors. |
| **Category Creation** | Stage 3+ markets with audience skepticism | Create a new category that the creator defines and owns | Higher effort but massive moat. Once established, competitors are followers. |

### The Mechanism Credibility Stack

A mechanism name alone is not enough. It must be supported by a credibility stack:

1. **The Name** -- proprietary, memorable, speakable
2. **The Explanation** -- 30-second plain-language description of how it works
3. **The Contrast** -- explicit comparison to old vehicles showing structural difference
4. **The Proof** -- at least one demonstration that the mechanism produces results (creator's own results, client results, logical proof, or demonstration)
5. **The Story** -- how the mechanism was discovered/created (connects to Narrative Architecture)

Without all five, the mechanism is a marketing gimmick rather than a strategic asset.

### Positioning at Different Market Maturity Stages

| Stage | Positioning Intensity | Key Message | Proof Requirement |
|-------|----------------------|-------------|-------------------|
| Stage 1 (Naive) | Light positioning | "Here's what's possible" | Promise is sufficient |
| Stage 2 (Aware) | Moderate positioning | "Here's why our way is better" | Comparison proof |
| Stage 3 (Skeptical) | Heavy positioning | "The reason everything else failed is X. Here's the structural fix." | Mechanism proof + results |
| Stage 4 (Exhausted) | Maximum positioning | "Everything you've been told is wrong. Here's an entirely new paradigm." | Paradigm demolition + new paradigm proof + extensive results |

## Brand Voice Knowledge

### Voice Consistency vs. Authenticity

The goal of voice extraction is NOT to create a rigid template. It is to identify the through-line -- the core vocal signature that appears across all platforms and formats when the creator is at their best. Some variation is natural and expected:

- **YouTube voice** may be more expansive, story-driven, and casual
- **Email voice** may be more direct, punchy, and action-oriented
- **Sales page voice** may be more structured, proof-heavy, and urgent
- **Social voice** may be more spontaneous, reactive, and compressed

The through-line is the set of patterns (vocabulary, humor style, authority markers, emotional range) that remain consistent across all of these.

### Common Voice Extraction Errors

1. **The Polish Error:** Making the voice sound more "professional" when the creator's audience fell in love with their rawness. If they say "this is BS" in their videos, the voice profile should reflect that -- not sanitize it to "this approach is flawed."

2. **The Performance Error:** Confusing the creator's best-performing content with their most authentic content. A viral video may have been scripted by someone else. Look for the voice that appears in unscripted moments, replies, and live sessions.

3. **The Average Error:** Averaging all content into a single flat voice. The voice profile should capture the range -- their teaching voice, their selling voice, their casual voice -- and identify what stays constant across all three.

4. **The Prescription Error:** Telling the creator how they SHOULD sound rather than documenting how they DO sound. The voice profile is a mirror, not a makeover.

### Voice-Market Fit

The extracted voice must be assessed for alignment with the ICP's communication preferences (from ICP Document Section 11: Information Diet). Key alignment checks:

- **Formality match:** Is the creator's voice at the same formality level the ICP consumes?
- **Authority match:** Does the creator's authority style match what the ICP respects?
- **Humor match:** Does the creator's humor land with this specific audience?
- **Vocabulary match:** Does the creator use the ICP's language or industry jargon the ICP would recognize?

If misalignment is detected, the voice profile notes it as a risk with a recommendation for a minor adjustment (never a reinvention).
