# Offer Architecture Frameworks

> Operational frameworks for competitive positioning, offer design, economics validation, and brand voice extraction. These are the specific methodologies this sub-agent applies when producing Positioning Documents, Offer Documents, and Brand Voice Profiles.

---

## Framework 1: Alen Sultanic 6-Phase Offer System

The complete offer architecture follows six sequential phases. Each phase builds on the previous. Skipping phases produces offers that look good on paper but fail in the market.

### Phase 1: Pre-Discovery

Before any research begins, establish the creator's starting position:
- What business do they have today (if any)?
- What is their personal experience with the problem they solve?
- What assets do they already possess (content, audience, testimonials, methodology)?
- What constraints exist (time, budget, team, technology)?

This phase prevents the common error of designing an offer the creator cannot actually deliver.

### Phase 2: Deep Audience Discovery

This phase is completed by the Market Research & ICP sub-agent. The Offer Architecture sub-agent consumes these outputs:
- ICP Document (13 sections, completeness >= 80/100)
- Market Research Brief (viability >= 50/100)
- Key inputs: limiting belief, awareness level, pain architecture, objections, buying intelligence, language patterns, what they have tried and failed

### Phase 3: Offer Architecture

Design the structural components:
- Transformation Statement (ICP + outcome + mechanism + timeframe - sacrifice)
- Tier structure (Downsell, Flagship, Upsell)
- Value Stack per tier (10:1 minimum perceived-value-to-price)
- Guarantee (matched to limiting belief)
- Momentum Loop (72-hour first result)

### Phase 4: Belief Building

Map the beliefs that must be installed before purchase:
- Belief 1: "This problem is solvable" (for the Hopeless)
- Belief 2: "This mechanism is different from what I've tried" (for everyone)
- Belief 3: "This can work for me specifically" (for the Hopeless/Worthless)
- Belief 4: "Now is the right time" (for procrastinators)
- Belief 5: "This creator can deliver" (for the Skeptical)

Each belief needs an installation method: content piece, case study, mechanism explanation, guarantee, or testimonial.

### Phase 5: Funnel Selection

Based on the offer tier and audience awareness level, select the appropriate conversion path:
- Level 1-2 awareness: Long-form content funnel (YouTube/blog --> lead magnet --> nurture --> webinar --> offer)
- Level 3 awareness: Webinar/challenge funnel (ad --> registration --> event --> offer)
- Level 4 awareness: Direct offer funnel (ad --> sales page --> checkout)
- Level 5 awareness: Simple offer page (email --> offer --> checkout)

### Phase 6: Copy Creation

This phase is NOT handled by the Offer Architecture sub-agent. It is handed off to downstream marketing and sales agents. The Offer Architecture sub-agent provides:
- Transformation Statement for headline development
- Belief Stack for content structure
- Objection map for copy handling
- Voice Profile for tonal alignment
- Value Stack for sales page structure

## Framework 2: Value Equation Framework

The master formula for evaluating and optimizing any offer's perceived value.

### The Formula

```
                  Dream Outcome  x  Perceived Likelihood of Achievement
Perceived Value = --------------------------------------------------------
                     Time Delay  x  Effort & Sacrifice Required
```

### Scoring Method

Rate each variable 1-10 for every offer tier:

| Variable | Score Range | How to Evaluate |
|----------|-----------|-----------------|
| **Dream Outcome** | 1 = vague improvement, 10 = specific, identity-level transformation | Is the outcome measurable? Does it change how the buyer sees themselves? Is it tied to the ICP's stated dream outcome? |
| **Perceived Likelihood** | 1 = "probably won't work," 10 = "this feels inevitable" | How strong is the proof? How specific is the mechanism? How relevant are the testimonials? How bold is the guarantee? |
| **Time Delay** (inverted) | 1 = years, 10 = days/immediate | How quickly does the buyer experience a first result? Is there a momentum loop? Are early wins designed in? |
| **Effort Required** (inverted) | 1 = massive effort, 10 = almost effortless | How many decisions does the buyer have to make? Are templates provided? Is there DFY support? Is the system simple? |

### Perceived Value Score

```
PV Score = (Dream Outcome x Perceived Likelihood) / (Time Delay x Effort)
```

**Interpretation:**
- PV Score > 3.0: Strong offer -- buyer perceives high value relative to cost
- PV Score 1.5-3.0: Acceptable offer -- may need optimization on one variable
- PV Score < 1.5: Weak offer -- needs structural redesign

### Optimization Priority

When an offer's PV Score is low, optimize in this order:
1. **Reduce Effort** (fastest impact) -- add templates, checklists, DFY elements
2. **Reduce Time Delay** (second fastest) -- add quick wins, momentum loops
3. **Increase Perceived Likelihood** (requires proof) -- add guarantee, testimonials, mechanism clarity
4. **Increase Dream Outcome** (requires repositioning) -- make the outcome more specific and identity-level

## Framework 3: USP Creation Framework

The Unique Selling Proposition is built from five components. All five must be present for the USP to be complete.

### The Five Building Blocks

```
USP = [Avatar] + [Desired Outcome] + [Unique Mechanism] + [Timeframe] + [Emotional Benefit]
```

| Block | Definition | Example (Fitness for Professionals) |
|-------|-----------|--------------------------------------|
| **Avatar** | The specific person this is for (from ICP) | "Busy professionals who work 50+ hours/week" |
| **Desired Outcome** | The measurable result they want | "Get back in athletic shape" |
| **Unique Mechanism** | The proprietary method/system | "Using the Adaptive Fitness Protocol" |
| **Timeframe** | When they can expect results | "In 90 days" |
| **Emotional Benefit** | How they will FEEL (identity-level) | "Without sacrificing career performance or family time" |

### Complete USP Example

"I help busy professionals who work 50+ hours a week get back in athletic shape in 90 days using the Adaptive Fitness Protocol -- without sacrificing career performance or family time."

### Validation Checklist

- [ ] Does it name a specific person (not "entrepreneurs" or "people who want to...")?
- [ ] Is the outcome measurable (not "improve" or "grow")?
- [ ] Is the mechanism named and ownable (not "my proven system")?
- [ ] Is the timeframe realistic and specific?
- [ ] Does the emotional benefit address the ICP's actual fear or desire?
- [ ] Could a competitor say the exact same thing? (If yes, it is not unique)

## Framework 4: 3-Tiered Offer Ladder

Every complete offer architecture includes three tiers. Each tier serves a different segment of the ICP at a different price point and commitment level.

### The Three Tiers

| Tier | Name | Price Range | What It Delivers | Who Buys It | Value Equation Focus |
|------|------|-------------|-----------------|-------------|---------------------|
| **Downsell** | Entry / Self-Paced | $497-$997 | Core methodology, templates, community access. Self-directed. | Budget-conscious buyers, DIY types, "let me try before committing" | Maximize dream outcome information. Accept higher buyer effort. |
| **Flagship** | Core / Guided | $3,000-$5,000 | Everything in Downsell + live coaching, accountability, hot seats, direct feedback. | The primary target. Ready to invest. Wants guidance and accountability. | Maximize perceived likelihood. Reduce buyer effort through guidance. |
| **Upsell** | Premium / Done-With-You or DFY | $10,000-$25,000+ | Everything in Flagship + 1-on-1 sessions, custom strategy, implementation support, priority access. | High-commitment buyers. Time-poor. Want maximum speed and minimum effort. | Maximize everything. Minimize time delay and effort. |

### Tier Design Principles

1. **Each tier is a complete solution.** The Downsell is not a crippled version of the Flagship. It is a real solution for people who prefer self-direction.
2. **The value gap between tiers is 3-5x.** Not 10x. If the gap is too large, it signals the lower tier is worthless.
3. **Higher tiers reduce the denominator.** Lower tiers increase the numerator. This is the structural principle -- you move from "more information" to "less effort."
4. **The Flagship is the anchor.** It is the tier most buyers should choose. The Downsell exists for those who are not ready. The Upsell exists for those who want more.
5. **Ascension should feel natural.** A buyer at the Downsell should organically see why the Flagship would accelerate their results. No hard-sell required.

## Framework 5: Positioning Framework

Competitive positioning follows a five-step process that transforms market research and ICP data into a defensible market position.

### Step 1: Old Vehicle Autopsy

For each of 3-5 methods the ICP has tried and failed (sourced from ICP Document Section 8):

| Element | What to Document |
|---------|-----------------|
| **Vehicle Name** | What they tried (the method, product, or approach) |
| **Promise Made** | What it claimed to deliver |
| **Why It Failed** | The STRUCTURAL reason it could not deliver -- not "they didn't try hard enough" |
| **Emotional Residue** | What belief the failure installed (shame, skepticism, hopelessness) |
| **ICP Language** | How they describe this failure in their own words (verbatim from ICP Document) |

The autopsy must use the ICP's language. Clinical descriptions fail to connect. If the ICP says "I threw money at a trainer who ghosted me," that exact language goes into the autopsy.

### Step 2: Category of One

Design a new category that the creator owns. This is not incremental positioning ("we're like X but better"). It is categorical positioning ("we're not X at all -- we're Y, which is a different thing entirely").

**The Five Tests:**

| Test | Question | Pass Criteria |
|------|----------|---------------|
| Category Test | Is this a different category, not just a better version? | Cannot be described as "[competitor] but better" |
| Contrast Test | Can someone immediately see how this differs from old vehicles? | A/B comparison is intuitive without explanation |
| Simplicity Test | Can a 12-year-old understand the core difference? | No jargon required |
| Believability Test | Does the ICP believe this could work for them? | Connects to at least one existing positive belief |
| Ownability Test | Could a competitor credibly claim this exact same thing? | If yes, it is not ownable -- redesign |

### Step 3: Mechanism Design

The mechanism is the proprietary method, system, or approach that makes the offer different. It is named, explainable, and ownable.

**Naming Convention:** [Descriptor] + [Domain Noun] + [Result Indicator]

Examples:
- "Adaptive Fitness Protocol" (adaptive + fitness + protocol)
- "Reverse Funnel Method" (reverse + funnel + method)
- "Deep Health Framework" (deep + health + framework)

**Requirements:**
- Explainable in 30 seconds or less
- Connected to the ICP's dominant limiting belief
- Supported by at least one proof element
- Not a generic concept anyone could claim

### Step 4: Core Belief Statement

```
"If I can get [specific ICP] to believe that [mechanism/approach] is the key
to [dream outcome], and that [old vehicle category] was the real reason they
haven't achieved [specific result] yet, then they will see [creator's offer]
as the obvious next step."
```

**Validation:** Contains exactly ONE belief. Names the ICP. References the mechanism. Points to the dream outcome. Positions old vehicles as the cause of past failure.

### Step 5: Narrative Architecture

| Stage | Element | Purpose |
|-------|---------|---------|
| 1. Origin | Creator's own struggle with the same problem | Establishes empathy and credibility |
| 2. Discovery | How the creator found/built the mechanism | Establishes authority and uniqueness |
| 3. Transformation | Creator's own results using the mechanism | Proof of concept |
| 4. Mission | Why the creator now teaches this | Purpose beyond profit |
| 5. Invitation | How the ICP can access the same transformation | Natural bridge to the offer |

## Framework 6: Brand Voice Extraction Method

Brand voice is extracted from existing content, never invented. The extraction follows a systematic process across six linguistic dimensions.

### Content Audit Requirements

Analyze a minimum of 10 pieces across 2+ platforms:
- Long-form video, short-form video, email/newsletter, sales pages, social posts, podcast/interviews, blog posts, comments/replies, course content, live sessions

For each piece: document title/link, format, platform, date, 3-5 voice observations, and 2-3 "most like them" phrases.

### Six Extraction Dimensions

1. **Sentence Structure:** Average length, variety, fragment usage, question frequency, command frequency, paragraph length, list vs. prose preference
2. **Vocabulary Patterns:** Signature words (their "tells"), avoided words, jargon level, profanity usage, coined terms, filler patterns
3. **Humor Style:** Self-deprecating, sarcastic, observational, dark, pun-based, absurdist, or no humor
4. **Metaphor & Analogy:** Source domains (sports, war, cooking, building), frequency, originality, extended vs. brief
5. **Emotional Range:** How they express excitement, frustration, empathy, confidence, vulnerability, urgency. Emotional floor and ceiling.
6. **Authority Markers:** How they establish credibility -- credentials, experience, results, name-dropping, certainty language

### Tone Spectrum Mapping

Plot the creator on seven axes:
```
Authoritative ──────────────────── Approachable
Provocative   ──────────────────── Nurturing
Formal        ──────────────────── Casual
Earnest       ──────────────────── Ironic
Dense         ──────────────────── Accessible
Fast-paced    ──────────────────── Measured
Prescriptive  ──────────────────── Exploratory
```

### Anti-Pattern Definition

Define 5+ anti-patterns -- what this voice NEVER sounds like. Each anti-pattern includes: the pattern, why it is wrong for this voice, an example of what NOT to write, and the correct alternative.

### Example Rewrites

Take 5+ generic marketing statements and rewrite them in the creator's voice. Cover different contexts: teaching, selling, storytelling, responding to objections, casual social.

## Framework 7: Economics Validation

Every offer must pass economic validation before it is approved. A strategically brilliant offer that loses money is not an offer -- it is a hobby.

### Core Metrics

| Metric | Formula | Minimum Threshold |
|--------|---------|-------------------|
| **LTV** | Average Price x Average Purchases x Retention Period | Must be calculated |
| **CAC** | Total Acquisition Cost / Number of Customers | Must be estimated |
| **LTV:CAC Ratio** | LTV / CAC | >= 3.0 |
| **Gross Margin** | (Revenue - COGS) / Revenue | >= 70% for digital products |
| **Break-Even** | Fixed Costs / (Price - Variable Cost per Unit) | Achievable within 90 days |
| **Payback Period** | CAC / Monthly Revenue per Customer | <= 3 months ideal |

### Conservative Assumptions

Use these defaults unless market data provides better estimates:

| Variable | Conservative Estimate | Notes |
|----------|----------------------|-------|
| Landing page conversion (cold) | 2-3% | For paid traffic from ads |
| Landing page conversion (warm) | 10-15% | For email list, retargeting |
| Email conversion | 1-2% of list per launch | Per launch, not per email |
| Refund rate | 10-15% | For digital products/courses |
| Upsell rate | 10-15% of buyers | For ascension to next tier |
| Retention (subscription) | 3-6 months average | For membership/community models |
| Ad CPM | $15-$40 | Varies by platform and niche |
| Click-through rate | 1-3% | For paid ads |

### Validation Process

1. Calculate LTV for each tier individually and as a blended average
2. Estimate CAC using market data, competitor ad analysis, and conservative assumptions
3. Calculate LTV:CAC -- must be >= 3.0
4. Calculate gross margin -- must be >= 70%
5. Calculate break-even in units and timeline
6. Calculate payback period
7. Stress-test: what happens if conversion is 50% of estimate? If refund rate doubles? If CAC is 2x estimate?

## Framework 8: Mechanism Naming

The mechanism name is one of the most important decisions in the entire positioning process. It must be memorable, ownable, and instantly communicative.

### Five Mechanism Types

| Type | Description | Naming Pattern | Example |
|------|-------------|---------------|---------|
| **Proprietary Process** | A step-by-step system unique to the creator | "[Number]-Step [Outcome] [System/Method/Process]" | "The 5-Phase Revenue Architecture" |
| **Unfair Advantage** | An insight or access point only the creator has | "The [Insider/Hidden/Untapped] [Domain] [Advantage/Edge]" | "The Insider Traffic Edge" |
| **Contrarian Approach** | Doing the opposite of conventional wisdom | "The [Reverse/Anti/Counter] [Domain] [Method/Strategy]" | "The Anti-Hustle Growth Method" |
| **Technology Leverage** | Using a tool, platform, or technology in a novel way | "The [Platform/Tech] [Leverage/Multiplier] [System]" | "The AI Content Multiplier System" |
| **Insight Edge** | A reframing or paradigm shift that changes everything | "The [Paradigm] [Shift/Framework/Principle]" | "The Constraint Advantage Framework" |

### Naming Rules

1. **3-4 words maximum.** Longer names are not mechanisms -- they are descriptions.
2. **Must feel proprietary.** If anyone could use the name, it is not a mechanism name.
3. **Must hint at the result.** The name should create curiosity about HOW it works.
4. **Must be speakable.** If it is awkward to say out loud, rename it.
5. **Must survive the "dinner party test."** If the creator cannot naturally say "I use the [Mechanism Name]" in a conversation, it is too jargony.

### Naming Process

1. Identify the mechanism type (from the five above)
2. Draft 5-10 name candidates following the naming pattern
3. Test each against the five rules
4. Test the top 3 with the ICP's language in mind -- would they use these words?
5. Select the name that best combines ownability, clarity, and ICP resonance

## Framework 9: Guarantee Design

The guarantee is where the creator's confidence meets the buyer's fear. It must be matched to the ICP's dominant limiting belief -- a generic "30-day money back" guarantee is strategically useless.

### Belief-Matched Guarantee Design

| Dominant Belief | Buyer's Hidden Fear | Guarantee Type | Structure | Example |
|----------------|--------------------|-|-|-|
| **Worthless** | "I'll fail because I'm not good enough" | **Performance Guarantee** | "If you don't [specific identity-shift result] in [time], [remedy]" | "If you don't land your first paying client in 90 days, I'll work with you 1-on-1 until you do -- at no extra cost." |
| **Helpless** | "I'll get confused and give up" | **Completion Guarantee** | "If you complete [specific actions] and don't get [result], [remedy]" | "Follow the 5-step system for 60 days. If you don't see measurable results, full refund plus you keep everything." |
| **Hopeless** | "This won't work for my specific situation" | **ROI/Reversal Guarantee** | "If [mechanism] doesn't produce [measurable result] for your specific situation, [remedy]" | "If the Adaptive Fitness Protocol doesn't improve your energy and fitness markers within 90 days, I'll refund 100% of your investment." |

### Guarantee Requirements

1. **Specific:** Not "satisfaction guaranteed." Name the exact result being guaranteed.
2. **Time-bound:** 30, 60, 90, or 180 days. Must be long enough for the mechanism to work.
3. **Conditional on reasonable effort:** The buyer must take specific, documented actions. This prevents abuse and signals that results require participation.
4. **Creator-confidence signaling:** The guarantee should feel bold enough that the buyer thinks "they must be really confident this works."
5. **Asymmetric risk:** The buyer risks time. The creator risks money. This asymmetry favors the buyer and lowers the perceived risk of purchase.

### Guarantee Anti-Patterns

| Anti-Pattern | Why It Fails |
|-------------|-------------|
| "30-day money-back guarantee" | Generic. Does not address any specific fear. Every competitor offers this. |
| "If you're not 100% satisfied..." | Vague. What does "satisfied" mean? The buyer cannot assess this before buying. |
| No guarantee at all | Signals either lack of confidence or lack of care. Especially toxic for Hopeless buyers. |
| Unconditional guarantee on high-ticket | Invites fraud. No conditions = no commitment filter. |
| "Results may vary" disclaimer | Legally necessary in some cases but should be balanced with bold guarantee language, not used as the guarantee itself. |
