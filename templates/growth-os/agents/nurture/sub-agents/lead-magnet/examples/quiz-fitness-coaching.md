# What's Your Body Recomp Score?
## The 2-Minute Assessment for Busy Professionals
### By Marcus Chen

Find out your body recomposition readiness type, your biggest hidden barrier, and the exact first step for your specific situation.

**Takes 2 minutes. Results are immediate and personalized.**

### Question 1: How would you describe your current fitness routine?

A) I haven't worked out consistently in over a year
B) I work out 1-2 times per week but without a structured plan
C) I follow a program (gym, classes, or app) but I'm not seeing the results I want
D) I used to be very fit but lost it due to work/life demands

**Scoring:** A = Starter, B = Inconsistent, C = Misdirected, D = Returner
**Psychological purpose:** Reveals their self-perception around fitness identity. Option D identifies the "fallen athlete" archetype who responds strongly to identity-restoration messaging.

---

### Question 2: What happens when you try to eat healthier?

A) I start strong but can't maintain it past 2-3 weeks
B) Work events, travel, and client dinners constantly derail me
C) I eat "clean" most of the time but I'm not seeing body composition changes
D) I've tried so many diets that I don't know what "healthy eating" means anymore

**Scoring:** A = Willpower-dependent, B = Lifestyle-constrained, C = Misinformed, D = Diet-fatigued
**Psychological purpose:** Identifies their relationship with nutrition and the specific friction point. This question addresses the Helpless belief pattern by naming their specific obstacle.

---

### Question 3: What's your biggest frustration with your body right now?

A) I carry excess weight around my midsection that won't budge
B) I'm not overweight but I look "soft" -- no muscle definition
C) I've lost weight before but always gain it back (and then some)
D) I feel tired all the time and my body doesn't perform like it used to

**Scoring:** A = Fat-focused, B = Composition-focused, C = Yo-yo pattern, D = Energy-focused
**Psychological purpose:** Surfaces their primary pain point for segmented follow-up. The yo-yo pattern (C) indicates a Hopeless archetype; the energy focus (D) often indicates hormonal/metabolic factors.

---

### Question 4: How much time can you realistically dedicate to training per week?

A) Less than 2 hours per week
B) 2-3 hours per week
C) 3-5 hours per week
D) I have time but I don't use it consistently

**Scoring:** A = Time-starved, B = Sweet spot, C = Available, D = Accountability gap
**Psychological purpose:** Sets realistic expectations and qualifies for the FitPath Pro program (which requires 3 hours/week). The "available but inconsistent" (D) answer reveals that time isn't their real barrier -- accountability is.

---

### Question 5: When you picture your ideal body in 6 months, what matters most?

A) Losing 20+ lbs and fitting into my old clothes
B) Looking strong and athletic -- visible muscle, flat stomach
C) Having consistent energy all day without caffeine dependency
D) Feeling confident with my shirt off -- at the pool, at the beach

**Scoring:** A = Scale-focused, B = Aesthetics-focused, C = Performance-focused, D = Confidence-focused
**Psychological purpose:** Identifies their primary motivation, which determines the language used in follow-up emails. Confidence-focused (D) indicates a Worthless-pattern ICP who responds to identity transformation messaging.

---

### Question 6: What's your relationship with alcohol?

A) I don't drink or drink very rarely
B) I have 2-5 drinks per week (wine with dinner, beers on weekends)
C) I drink socially 3-4 times per week and it's part of my professional life
D) I drink more than I'd like to and it's probably affecting my health

**Scoring:** A = Non-factor, B = Manageable, C = Integrated, D = Priority
**Psychological purpose:** Alcohol is the unspoken barrier for most professional men. This question normalizes it and prevents the "but I can't give up drinking" objection before it forms. The quiz results address alcohol within each type.

---

### Question 7: Which statement resonates most with you?

A) "I know what to do, I just can't seem to do it consistently"
B) "I don't actually know what the right approach is -- there's too much conflicting information"
C) "I've tried the right things but my body doesn't respond like other people's"
D) "I could probably figure it out but I don't have time to research and experiment"

**Scoring:** A = Helpless (execution gap), B = Helpless (information overload), C = Hopeless (uniqueness bias), D = Helpless (time scarcity)
**Psychological purpose:** This is the key diagnostic question. It directly identifies the Limiting Belief Triad pattern. Answers A/B/D indicate Helpless; Answer C indicates Hopeless. This drives the entire personalization of the results page and follow-up sequence.

### Result Type 3: The Misinformed Grinder
**Score Profile:** Primarily C answers across questions 1, 3, and 7
**Percentage of takers:** ~20%

**Your Result: The Misinformed Grinder**

You're actually putting in work -- but you're getting diminishing returns because your approach has structural flaws. You're doing too much cardio, eating too few calories, or following a program designed for a different body type and lifestyle than yours.

**Your strengths:**
- You have the work ethic and consistency that most people lack
- You're already in the habit of training -- we just need to redirect your effort
- Your frustration is actually a sign that you're ready for a better approach (complacent people don't get frustrated)

**Your blind spots:**
- You're conflating effort with progress. Working harder is not the answer; working differently is.
- You may be in a chronic calorie deficit that has downregulated your metabolism
- Your cardio-heavy approach is likely elevating cortisol and burning muscle along with fat

**Your biggest hidden barrier:**
Metabolic adaptation. If you've been in a calorie deficit for more than 8-12 weeks without a strategic refeed or maintenance phase, your body has adapted to the lower intake. Your metabolism has slowed to match your reduced calories, and additional cutting won't break through the plateau -- it will make it worse.

### Result Type 4: The Fallen Athlete
**Score Profile:** D answers on questions 1 and 3, mixed elsewhere
**Percentage of takers:** ~15%

**Your Result: The Fallen Athlete**

You used to be fit. Maybe in college, maybe in your 20s, maybe even your early 30s. You know what it feels like to be in shape, and the gap between then and now is a daily source of frustration. Career, family, and age have changed your body -- and you're not sure the old version is recoverable.

**Your strengths:**
- You have muscle memory (literal -- your muscle fibers retain the ability to regrow faster than they initially developed)
- You know what good training feels like, which means you'll progress faster than a true beginner
- Your identity includes "someone who is fit" -- we're restoring, not creating

**Your blind spots:**
- You're comparing your current self to your 25-year-old self, which isn't fair or productive
- Your hormonal profile has changed, and the training that worked at 25 won't work the same at 35-45
- You may be trying to jump back to advanced training too quickly, risking injury

**Your biggest hidden barrier:**
Hormonal change. Testosterone peaks in the mid-20s and declines roughly 1% per year after 30. This doesn't mean you can't build an incredible physique -- it means the method has to account for the change.
